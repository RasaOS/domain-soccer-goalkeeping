# Distribution

Distribution is everything the goalkeeper does to restart play once the ball
is in their possession or at their feet: kicking the ball out of the hand
(**punt**, **dropkick**), kicking a moving ball off the ground (the
**backpass**), and **throwing** for distance and accuracy. It is the one
category where the goalkeeper turns from last defender into first attacker.

A framing note before the technique: punting and dropkicking are the two
distribution techniques that **"take several years and many matches to get a
good feel for"** (Robo). Distribution is therefore a long-horizon skill — it
is trained from a young age but matured slowly, and the developing keeper is
given a safety-valve rule of thumb (**"when in doubt, kick it out"**) until the
judgement is built. The Plan reflects this: distribution is **not** a syllabus
item at U9-U12 (where the focus is footwork, catching, footskills, judging
balls, diving, positioning), enters at **U13-U15 as "Distribution,"** and
becomes **"Advanced Distribution" at U16-U18**.

Robo is the source for all four kicking/throwing techniques and their cues. The
Plan adds the age-band placement and the indirect-free-kick rule; the ECNL
Handbook contributes the verbal organizing tools that turn distribution into
build-up. See also [playing with feet](playing-with-feet.md) for in-run and
back-pass play, [psychology & mentality](psychology-mentality.md) for the
decision discipline behind "kick it out," and the [glossary](../vocabulary.md)
for term definitions.

## "When in doubt, kick it out" — the developing-keeper rule of thumb

Robo states it plainly: **"when in doubt, kick it out" should be a guiding rule
of thumb for developing goalkeepers.** The point is not that clearing is the
ideal outcome — it is that a young keeper without secure passing judgement under
pressure is safer eliminating the danger than gambling on a played-out pass that
can be intercepted in front of goal.

The same source immediately qualifies it as a developmental stage, not a
permanent ceiling: **"as the GK matures he/she will have to develop sound
passing techniques from deep positions on the field."** So the rule is a floor,
not a model. Train the safe clearance first; layer in build-up as the keeper's
feet and reading mature.

The ECNL/Plan analogue at the team-organization level is **"INTO TOUCH"** — the
command used "when the defense is not in position to build a counter or play out
of the back": express urgency and clear it out. Same instinct, expressed as a
command to the back line rather than a personal default.

## Throwing

Throwing is the most accurate form of distribution and the fastest to start an
attack at short-to-mid range. Robo calls it **"a skill that may need work but
comes quickly with practice."** Three families of throw are in common use —
**roll**, **javelin**, and **overhand** — distinguished by trajectory and range;
Robo's mechanics describe the throwing action and how to grade delivery height.

**Throwing mechanics (Robo):**

- Like punting, **take steps toward the target.**
- **Plant to throw with the knee of the planting foot bent.**
- **Prepare the ball behind the body with the palm up.**
- As the shoulders come forward, **the throwing arm comes forward.**
- **As the ball leaves the hand the palm should "open" up** — palm and fingers
  facing down.
- **The lower the delivery, the more the non-throwing leg should be flexed and
  the lower the shoulders should be to the ground.** The ball should leave the
  hand directly, **without a lot of "side-ways" spin.**

**Reading the throw families against those cues:**

- **Roll** — the lowest, most controllable delivery. Knee deeply flexed,
  shoulders low to the ground, ball released directly onto the grass with no
  side spin so it tracks true to the receiver's feet. Best for short, safe
  distribution to an unmarked teammate's feet.
- **Javelin** — a flatter, faster throw with greater range than the roll,
  released from beside the head with a quicker arm action; the ball travels low
  and hard to a teammate in space.
- **Overhand** (the bowling/sling action) — the longest throw, releasing the
  ball with the "palm-opens-down" finish over a more upright body to clear
  distance to a wide teammate or a switch.

> Note: Robo describes the throwing *action* and the height-grading principle in
> detail but does not name "roll / javelin / overhand" as such. The three named
> families are the standard coaching taxonomy for that range of deliveries; the
> mechanics above are verbatim-faithful to Robo, the labels are the conventional
> grouping. The sources do not provide separate per-throw cue lists.

**Target practice (Robo):** **practice throwing the ball onto the foot of a
moving teammate.** Robo flags the common failure mode explicitly — **throws with
a slight loft that bounce in front of or to the side of a teammate are very
difficult to control.** A good throw arrives flat and to the foot, not lofted
and bouncing.

## Punt

The punt is the long-range kick out of the hand, producing a **high, arching
ball** for maximum distance. It is one of the two techniques Robo says take
"several years and many matches to get a good feel for."

**Approach and momentum (Robo):**

- **Build momentum for contact** by starting with a long step then taking
  several shorter steps to the contact point.
- Young keepers **may find holding the ball with both hands easier than moving
  forward with one** — let the technique catch up to the carry.

**Strike mechanics (Robo):**

- **Plant the non-kicking foot with the leg slightly bent.**
- **Move the ball forward on a plane away from the body at about hip height or
  just under.**
- **Hold the ball steady then drop it** in front of the body.
- **Look at the ball and make contact behind and underneath it.**
- **The kicking foot should be pointed** and **the GK should come forward onto
  the kicking foot.**

**Good result (Robo):** a **high, arching ball that "turns over" in mid-flight
and extends its distance.** The mid-flight turn-over is the diagnostic of a
cleanly struck punt.

## Dropkick (half-volley)

The dropkick is the long, **driven, low-trajectory** kick out of the hand — same
family as the punt, opposite flight. It is the second of Robo's two
"several-years" techniques.

**Mechanics (Robo):**

- **Same initial technique** as the punt (the approach and momentum build are
  shared).
- **Plant the non-kicking foot**, **shoulder comes forward**, and **the hand
  holding the ball gets close to the ground.**
- **Drop the ball**, then **lean the shoulders backward**, and **the kicking
  foot immediately comes through the back of the ball.**
- **Toes of the kicking foot locked down.**
- Result: **the ball is driven in a low trajectory** — a fast, flat ball that
  reaches a target quickly and is harder to read out of the keeper's hands than
  a lofted punt.

The contrast to hold onto: punt = look at the ball, contact **behind and
underneath**, foot **pointed**, come **forward** → high and arching. Dropkick =
ball **close to the ground**, lean shoulders **back**, foot **through the back**
of the ball with **toes locked down** → low and driven.

## Kicking a moving ball off the ground (the backpass)

This is the back-pass technique — a teammate plays the ball back and the keeper
kicks it (now a foot skill, no hands permitted). Robo notes the cues here are
**"likely most familiar to the team coach of any technique in the document,"**
because it is ordinary field-player passing under goalkeeper-specific pressure.
See [playing with feet](playing-with-feet.md), which treats this skill as part
of the keeper's outfield game.

**Reading the pass and timing (Robo):**

- **Hold position as the ball is played back** to **assess the pressure on the
  back-pass and the quality of the pass.** Holding lets minor adjustments be
  made as the ball arrives.
- **Meet the ball early if the back-pass is being contested** by an opponent —
  don't let it run if pressure is closing.

**Strike selection (Robo):**

- **Smooth back-passes can be played with the instep and driven away.**
- **Balls that come in on the ground and bounce might play best with the side of
  the foot.**

This is the situation where **"when in doubt, kick it out"** applies most
directly. Under genuine pressure on a contested back-pass, the developing keeper
clears (or plays it into touch) rather than attempting a composed played-out
pass.

## Goal kicks

The sources do **not** give a dedicated goal-kick technique section. The
mechanics map onto the **"kicking a moving ball off the ground"** technique
above — the goal kick is a dead ball struck off the ground from the six-yard
box, played with the instep for a driven ball over distance (the same
instep-drive Robo describes for a smooth back-pass).

The strategic frame the sources do supply:

- **Robo:** the maturing keeper must **"develop sound passing techniques from
  deep positions on the field"** — the long-horizon goal-kick / restart skill
  the "kick it out" rule is the placeholder for.
- **ECNL / Plan:** a goal kick (or any restart from the back) is the moment to
  use the build-up commands — **"(KEEPER'S) HOME"** to invite the ball back and
  start build-up, **"SWITCH"** to send it quickly to the far side, **"SWING"**
  to drop it to a teammate who then switches the point of attack, and
  **"TIME"** to tell a receiving teammate there is little pressure and they can
  play with their feet. When the back line cannot build, **"INTO TOUCH"** —
  clear it.

## When to distribute fast vs. keep possession

The sources frame this less as a kicking technique and more as a **decision** —
read the pressure, then choose the restart. Drawing the threads together:

**Distribute fast (clear / counter quickly) when:**

- The keeper is **in doubt** under pressure — **"when in doubt, kick it out"**
  (Robo). The default for a developing keeper.
- The **back-pass is being contested** — **meet the ball early** and clear
  rather than letting an opponent close (Robo).
- The **defense "is not in position to build a counter or play out of the
  back"** — **"INTO TOUCH,"** express urgency, clear it (ECNL/Plan).
- A throw or dropkick can **release a moving teammate** before the opposition
  resets — the fast, accurate options (roll/javelin throw, driven dropkick)
  start an attack the lofted punt cannot.

**Keep possession / play out (slower, build-up) when:**

- There is **little pressure** — **"TIME"** tells the receiving teammate they
  can play with their feet (ECNL/Plan).
- The keeper can **organize a build-up** — **"(KEEPER'S) HOME"** to invite the
  ball back, then **"SWITCH" / "SWING"** to move the point of attack
  (ECNL/Plan).
- The keeper has **matured into sound deep-passing technique** — Robo's explicit
  endpoint: the developed keeper plays out, the developing keeper clears.

The honest read across the sources: **none of the three prescribes a percentage
or a hard rule for fast-vs-keep.** Robo gives the developmental default (clear
when in doubt, build later); the ECNL/Plan vocabulary supplies the *commands* a
keeper uses once they've decided to keep possession, but leaves the decision to
the keeper's reading of pressure. Treat the choice as a judgement that matures
with the keeper, gated by the "kick it out" floor.

## Indirect free kick conceded inside the box — kill the play

A distribution-adjacent rule from the ECNL Handbook and the Plan: if the keeper
commits an offence inside their own box that concedes an **indirect free kick**
(holding the ball with the hands more than **six seconds**; touching it again
with the hands after releasing it before another player touches it; handling a
ball **deliberately kicked back by a teammate**; or handling a ball received
**directly from a teammate's throw-in**), the keeper **"must kill the play."**

The instruction (ECNL/Plan): **kick the ball as far as they can to force a
whistle** — and **"if it draws a yellow card, so be it."** Build a wall covering
as much of the goal as possible; because the kick is indirect it must be touched
first, which is the keeper's cue to **charge the ball to cut the angle** while
the wall stays behind to cover. (Full set-piece organization lives in
[aerial play & crosses](aerial-crosses.md) and the
[communication syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md).)

> Robo does not cover this situation; it is ECNL/Plan content.

## Age-band placement (Plan)

- **U9-U12** — distribution is **not** a listed syllabus item. The base is built
  first: footwork, catching, footskills, judging balls (air / ground / 50/50),
  diving, positioning/angles, testing.
- **U13-U15** — **"Distribution"** enters the syllabus, alongside communication,
  back-to-bar, set plays, and fitness.
- **U16-U18** — **"Advanced Distribution,"** part of the advanced syllabus
  across all categories.

This ladder is consistent with Robo's "takes several years to get a good feel
for": the punt and dropkick are matured over the U13-U18 window, not rushed at
the youngest ages. See the
[development framework](../framework/development-framework.md) for the full
age-band structure and the [training methodology](../framework/training-methodology.md)
for how distribution is grooved (Simple-to-Complex / Whole-Part-Whole), and the
[drill library](../drills/library.md) for distribution practice setups.
