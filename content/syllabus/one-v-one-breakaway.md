# 1v1 / Breakaway

The 1v1 / breakaway is the moment a goalkeeper leaves the safety of the line
to confront an attacker who has, or is about to receive, the ball in space
behind the defense. It is the highest-leverage decision in the position:
the choice to engage, the timing of the advance, the body shape on contact,
and the judgment of when to hold are all compressed into a second or two.

All three sources treat this as primarily a **decision-making** problem, not
a reflex problem. Robo ("The GK Trainer") covers it as the technique of
**diving at an opponent's feet** plus the broader positional "can I win the
ball?" thought process. ECNL and Plan converge on a shared rules-of-thumb
framework — **steal ground, stalk, meet at the highest point, delay** — and
add the **through-ball decision** as a closely related read. This file
synthesizes all three. For the underlying movement see
[footwork](footwork-movement.md), for the diving mechanics see
[shot-stopping & diving](shot-stopping-diving.md), and for the broader
question of where to start see [positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md).

---

## 1. The decision to engage

Before any technique, the goalkeeper must answer one question. Robo states it
plainly: when coming off the line, the goalkeeper must think **"Can I win the
ball?"** — and crucially, this is **a consideration, not a commandment**. The
full thought process has three branches:

1. **Hold** — stay set and react to the shot.
2. **Come part way, take a new angle, and hold** — narrow the angle without
   fully committing.
3. **Come all the way and win the ball outright.**

Robo lists the factors that decide between them:

- The **total distance** the goalkeeper has to travel to win it.
- Whether a **teammate can win it** or is closer to the ball.
- Whether there are **obstacles** (opponents and teammates) impeding the path.

ECNL and Plan frame the same read on a **through ball** (see §6 below): the
goalkeeper must **read the ball and decide whether they can win it, OR steal
ground and stalk**. The unifying principle across all three: a commitment to
come out must be a genuine read of the situation, not a reflex to charge.

**Once committed, stay committed.** ECNL and Plan are emphatic: *"If the GK
decides to go out, they have now committed and must stick to their decision."*
Half-committing — starting to come and then stopping — is the worst outcome,
because it surrenders both the angle and the ball.

---

## 2. Calling it: KEEPER!

When the goalkeeper decides to come win the ball, the verbal response is
**"KEEPER!"** (Robo). This is not optional noise — it is **a command of
courtesy and tactics**:

- It tells **teammates** the decision is made, so they take up positions of
  cover behind the advancing goalkeeper.
- It tells **opponents** a physical challenge is coming.

ECNL and Plan reinforce that the KEEPER call is **always two syllables** —
never allow the keeper to simply yell "Keep." Conversely, if the keeper
*cannot* win it and wants a teammate to take it, the call is **"AWAY!"** See
the [communication syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md) for the
full command vocabulary and the four-stage progression.

---

## 3. Timing the advance: steal ground, only on the touch

The single most important timing cue, shared verbatim across ECNL and Plan:

> **Steal ground by taking steps forward ONLY once the opponent dribbles the
> ball away from their foot.**

This is the **Steal Ground** technique: while an opponent is dribbling, the
goalkeeper steps toward them, taking ground *while the ball is away from the
feet*. Closing while the ball is tight to the attacker's foot invites the
attacker to knock it past or chip it; closing on the loose touch lets the
goalkeeper cut the angle and shorten the distance safely.

**Rule of Thumb** (ECNL and Plan, identical): *the faster a player is
dribbling, the more likely they are to try to beat the goalkeeper 1v1; as a
player slows down, they prepare for the shot.* Read the attacker's speed to
anticipate intent — a sprinting dribbler is looking to go *around* or
*through*; a decelerating one is loading a shot.

For the underlying footwork mechanics — **Stalk** (keeping the feet in a low
ready position while tracking the dribbler) and the **Power Step** that
launches a dive — see [footwork & movement](footwork-movement.md).

---

## 4. Meet at the highest point — and protect against the chip first

ECNL and Plan organize the advance as a sequence of **positions**: the
goalkeeper moves through **GK 1st → 2nd → 3rd → 4th position**, tracking the
attacker's corresponding positions, with the goal of **meeting the opponent at
the highest point possible**. Two cues govern this:

- **The chip is the most important worry in the beginning.** Early in the
  advance, the goalkeeper's first priority is to not get chipped — the ball
  lifted over the head and into the goal (the **Chip** failure term). This
  constrains how aggressively and how high the keeper can come.
- **Meet the opponent at the highest point possible — *without giving up the
  chip*.** The two demands are in tension: coming higher (closer to the
  attacker, further from goal) cuts the shooting angle, but coming too high
  too early leaves the goalkeeper vulnerable over the top. The skill is
  advancing to the highest point that still protects the goal behind.

This is the heart of breakaway positioning: a controlled, staged advance that
trades angle for chip-exposure and stops at the optimal balance point. See
[positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md) for how the starting position
(1–2 yards off the end line in the final third, per Robo) feeds into this.

---

## 5. Body shape on contact: long barrier, spread, and making yourself big

When the goalkeeper does commit to win the ball at the attacker's feet, Robo
gives the technique in detail. It is, in Robo's words, *"similar to a simple
dive for a low ball"* — see [shot-stopping & diving](shot-stopping-diving.md)
for the base low-dive mechanics. The breakaway-specific cues (Robo, 7 cues):

1. **Most often associated with a goalkeeper coming off the line to deal with
   a through ball**; technically similar to a simple dive for a low ball.
2. **Push off the foot nearest the ball.**
3. **The decision to dive should be made when the ball is played away from the
   oncoming opponent** — i.e., dive on the loose touch, the same window the
   Steal Ground cue identifies in §3.
4. **Keep the top elbow up** to offer greater protection of the head.
5. **Press the hands into the ground.** It is critical that the hands press
   the ball into the ground — this helps **hold the ball if challenged**.
6. **After the opposition goes by the goalkeeper, pull the ball into the
   chest.** (Not before — see the next cue.)
7. **Do NOT pull the top leg across the body too early**, as this tends to
   **push the shoulders backward** — which collapses the barrier and rolls the
   keeper away from the ball.

The shape this produces is a **long barrier on the ground behind the ball**:
the keeper spread low across the attacker's path, hands pinning the ball,
top elbow shielding the head, body big and committed through the contact
rather than flinching away. "Making yourself big" is the implicit goal of the
whole technique — present the largest possible obstacle between ball and goal,
and hold it through the collision.

> **Sources note.** The detailed *diving-at-the-feet* mechanics (the 7 cues
> above) are Robo's alone. ECNL and Plan describe the *decision and timing* of
> the breakaway (steal ground, meet at highest point, delay) but do not break
> down the dive-at-feet technique. The full picture comes from combining them:
> ECNL/Plan tell you *when and whether* to go; Robo tells you *how* to win the
> ball once you do.

## 6. The through-ball read

The breakaway's close cousin is the **through ball** — a pass played *through*
the defense into space behind it, before any attacker has clear possession.
ECNL and Plan treat this as its own decision (the **Through-Ball Decision**):

- **Read the ball and decide:** can I **win it** (step and smother), or should
  I **steal ground and stalk** and prepare for a shot or 1v1?
- **Be aware of backside opponents making runs through the defense** — find
  the **seams** the ball can get through. The threat is not only the ball in
  front of you but the runner you cannot see.
- **If you decide to go out, you have committed — stick to the decision**
  (same commitment rule as §1).
- If you do *not* commit to smother, fall back to **steal ground / stalk and
  prepare for the shot or 1v1** — i.e., convert the through-ball read into the
  breakaway read of §3–§5.

The two situations form a continuum: a through ball the keeper cannot reach
becomes a breakaway, and the same toolkit — read, steal ground on the touch,
meet at the highest point, hold or dive — applies.

---

## 7. When to hold: delay is the true job

The most important strategic point in this category, shared by ECNL and Plan
in nearly identical wording:

> **The goalkeeper must keep their feet as long as possible. The true job is
> to DELAY and allow teammates to recover and help.**

This reframes the breakaway. The goalkeeper's instinct is to *win* the ball;
the sources insist the higher-value outcome is often to **stay on the feet,
stay big, and buy time** — narrowing the angle, stalking, and delaying the
attacker until defenders recover to outnumber the threat. Going to ground
early (committing to a dive) is a one-shot decision; staying up keeps every
option alive.

Robo's positional teaching aligns: the default is to **hold and react** —
*"anticipating what might happen, before it happens, usually leads to
failure."* When set and holding close to goal or after coming off the line
(through ball), Robo specifies the **hold posture**: knees slightly bent, and
the **hands come down low to protect a shot from going under the body**.

So the decision hierarchy on a breakaway is:

1. **Hold and delay** if teammates can recover — keep your feet, stay big,
   stalk, force the attacker wide or onto a defender.
2. **Steal ground and re-angle** if you can shorten the distance safely (only
   on the loose touch).
3. **Commit and win it** (dive at the feet, §5) only when the read is clear:
   the ball is played away from the attacker, the distance is winnable, no
   teammate is closer, and the path is clear.

Holding is not passivity — it is the active, disciplined choice to make the
attacker beat a big, set, patient goalkeeper rather than handing them an easy
decision by diving in early.

## Quick-reference: the breakaway sequence

| Phase | Cue | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Decide** | "Can I win the ball?" — hold / part-way & re-angle / come all the way. Weigh distance, teammate proximity, obstacles. | Robo |
| **Commit** | Once you decide to go out, you are committed — stick to it. | ECNL, Plan |
| **Call** | "KEEPER!" (two syllables) to claim; "AWAY!" to release. | Robo, ECNL, Plan |
| **Time the advance** | Steal ground ONLY when the ball is away from the attacker's foot. Faster dribbler → more likely to try to beat you; slowing → loading a shot. | ECNL, Plan |
| **Advance** | Move through 1st→2nd→3rd→4th position; meet at the highest point — but protect the chip first. | ECNL, Plan |
| **Win at the feet** | Push off the foot nearest the ball; dive when ball is played away from the opponent; top elbow up; press hands into the ground; pull ball to chest *after* opponent passes; don't pull top leg across too early. | Robo |
| **Hold / delay** | Keep your feet as long as possible — the true job is to delay and let teammates recover. Hold low, hands down to stop the ball going under. | ECNL, Plan, Robo |
