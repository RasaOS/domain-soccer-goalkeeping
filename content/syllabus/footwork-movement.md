# Footwork, Movement & Set Position

Footwork is the engine under every other goalkeeping technique. Robo names
it as one of the three goalkeeping essentials alongside catching and
positioning, and states plainly that "the hallmark of good catching comes
from good lateral footwork." ECNL and Plan list footwork **first in every
age band** and make it a staple of every warm-up. Get the feet right and
the hands, the dive, and the angle all follow; get the feet wrong and
nothing downstream can be saved cleanly.

This file covers the ready/set stance, the mechanics of the first step,
shuffling vs. sprinting across goal, recovery, and the act of getting
"set" before a shot. The three sources approach the same problem with two
overlapping vocabularies — Robo describes the mechanics in prose; ECNL and
Plan give them named, glossary-defined terms (Ready Position, Set Yourself,
Stalk, Steal Ground, Power Step). They agree on the substance. Where they
diverge or where only one covers something, it is flagged.

For how footwork finishes into a save, see [shot-stopping & diving](shot-stopping-diving.md).
For where the footwork is taking you, see [positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md).
Terms in **bold** are defined in the [glossary](../vocabulary.md).

## The Ready / Set Stance

The stance is the platform the goalkeeper reacts from. The two source
families describe it from two distances.

**The base ready position (ECNL & Plan — "Goalie Stance / Ready Position"):**

- Two hands down to the **sides** of the body.
- Knees bent; body in a squat position.
- Feet shoulder-width apart.
- No movement — just attention to the ball.

ECNL and Plan word this identically. Note the hands are at the **sides**,
not stiffly out front; the stance is a relaxed, balanced squat ready to
explode in any direction.

**Robo's distance-graded stance.** Robo does not use the term "Ready
Position" but describes the same posture and — usefully — adjusts it by how
far the ball is from goal:

- **Ball away from goal (16–24 yards):** be upright; shoulders rolled
  forward; hands hanging downward and slightly tense (not stiff); feet
  spread about shoulder width; weight balanced on the **ball of each
  foot**.
- **Ball/attacker close to goal (3, 4 or 5 yards), or the GK has come off
  the line on a through ball:** hold with the knees slightly bent and the
  hands **down low** to protect a shot from going under the body.

The synthesis: shoulder-width feet, bent knees, weight on the balls of the
feet, hands relaxed and low. As the ball gets closer, sink lower and bring
the hands down to cover the low shot. This is the same posture ECNL/Plan
call Ready Position, refined by Robo for proximity.

## Getting "Set" Before the Shot

Being **set** means stationary, balanced, and ready the instant the ball is
struck. All three sources treat this as non-negotiable: you cannot react
well off moving feet.

- **ECNL / Plan — "Set Yourself":** *stop moving and get into Ready
  Position.* The cue is literal — feet stop, body squats, attention locks
  on the ball, **before** the shot is taken.
- **Robo — stepping out of the sprint to get set:** the key to finishing a
  movement across goal "is to take a step sideways to come out of the
  sprint — this slows momentum and gets the GK 'set' for the shot coming
  onto goal." (See *Recovering & Getting Set* below.)

Robo ties the set position to the mental side of the position: a mature
goalkeeper "appears calm just as the ball is hit," and a developed
goalkeeper asked what they think about when set and holding answers
"absolutely nothing." Being physically set enables holding and reacting
rather than guessing — see [hold-and-react](#hold-and-react-the-movement-discipline)
below and [psychology & mentality](psychology-mentality.md).

## The First Step — Mechanics of Moving to the Ball

This is the most load-bearing footwork detail in the whole syllabus, and
Robo covers it in the most depth. The principle: **the first step is taken
by the foot closest to the ball.**

Robo's first-step rules:

- The first step should be taken by **the foot that is closest to the
  ball**.
- The first step should be **as long as possible** and should **adjust to
  the serve** — a sharper or wider serve demands a longer or differently
  angled first step.
- The **foot farthest from the serve should smoothly follow the lead
  foot** (lead foot → trailing foot).
- If more than one step is needed, take additional steps to **get the body
  behind the ball**.
- Throughout, footwork should keep the body **square to the line of
  flight** (the path of the struck/served ball) and keep the body "in
  line" with the field of play.

For a ball played **away from the body**, Robo calls the first step "the
most critical factor" in the technique: push off with the foot closest to
the ball — this is **lead away**. *If the ball is shot to the GK's right,
lead away with the right foot.* The push-off foot and direction are
dictated entirely by which side the ball is on.

**ECNL / Plan — the "Power Step."** ECNL and Plan compress the move-to-a-
struck-ball into a single named term: when a shot has been taken, **the
foot closest to the ball steps laterally toward the ball.** They note this
step is used "in the process of diving" — it is the bridge between footwork
and the dive. This is the same first-step-by-the-nearest-foot principle
Robo describes, named and pointed specifically at the save. See
[shot-stopping & diving](shot-stopping-diving.md), where the Power Step
launches the Collapse / Step / Extension dive ladder.

Convergence to note: Robo's "push off with the foot closest to the ball /
lead away" and ECNL/Plan's "Power Step" are the **same mechanic**. Robo
also uses "power step" for a different thing — the top leg coming across
the body during an extended dive — so the term is overloaded across sources.
In this syllabus, **Power Step** (ECNL/Plan sense) = the lateral first step
by the near foot toward the ball; Robo's diving "power step" is documented
in [shot-stopping & diving](shot-stopping-diving.md).

## Shuffling vs. Sprinting Across Goal

Robo is the source for the two modes of covering ground across the face of
goal. Both share one rule: **the goalkeeper always faces the field of
play.**

**Shuffling:**

- Moving across the goal while **facing the field of play**.
- Used for shorter adjustments and to set good near-post / far-post angles.
- Keeps the body square and the eyes on the ball the whole way — the GK is
  ready to set and react at any point in the shuffle.

**Sprinting (across the goal):**

- Used to cover **more ground, faster** than a shuffle allows.
- Achieved by sprinting while **keeping the shoulders turned** so the GK
  still faces the field of play throughout.
- Because the GK is now moving fast and not square, it **must be finished**
  with a recovery step to get set (next section).

Decision cue (Robo): shuffle when the distance is short enough to stay
square and balanced; sprint when the ball has traveled far enough — square
across the field or wide to a post — that a shuffle would arrive too late.
Robo stresses that strong footwork for near-post / far-post situations is
"essential to establish good angles" — the footwork mode is chosen to
serve the angle, not the other way around. See
[positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md).

## Recovering & Getting Set (Coming Out of a Sprint)

A sprint across goal leaves the keeper moving and unbalanced — fatal if the
shot arrives mid-stride. Robo's recovery cue closes the loop:

> The key to finishing a sprint across the field is to **take a step
> sideways to come out of the sprint** — this slows momentum and gets the
> GK "set" for the shot coming onto goal.

So the full sequence is: **sprint (shoulders turned, facing field) →
sideways recovery step → set → react.** The sideways step kills the lateral
momentum that would otherwise carry the keeper past the line of the shot.
This is the same end-state ECNL/Plan call **Set Yourself** — arriving back
in Ready Position before the ball is struck.

After **catching**, Robo adds a related footwork point: footwork after the
catch keeps the body "in line" with the field of play, ready for the next
action. Movement does not stop when the ball is secured. See
[handling & catching](handling-catching.md).

## Stalk & Steal Ground — Footwork in Live, Moving Situations

ECNL and Plan add a second footwork vocabulary for when the keeper is
moving against a live opponent (a dribbler, a breakaway, a through ball)
rather than reacting to a struck shot. These are about **cutting angles and
managing distance**, not launching a save.

- **Stalk:** in a **LOW** Ready Position, the GK keeps their feet while
  following the opponent with the ball. ECNL/Plan note this "requires
  better-than-average footwork to maintain a low center of gravity while
  holding steady balance during movement." Stalking is how the keeper
  stays balanced and ready while an attacker is still in control of the
  ball — never committing, always able to set and react.
- **Steal Ground:** while an opponent is dribbling, the GK **steps toward
  them, taking ground while the ball is away from the feet.** This lets the
  GK cut the angle and shorten the distance to the ball. The timing rule
  (sharpened in the breakaway context): **take steps forward only once the
  opponent dribbles the ball away from their foot** — advance on the touch,
  hold when the ball is at their feet.

Together, Stalk and Steal Ground are the footwork expression of ECNL's core
philosophy: **be proactive — "eliminate the danger before it happens. If
you only react, then you have to make a save."** The keeper uses these
moving-footwork tools to take ground and cut angle *before* the shot, so
the save is smaller or unnecessary. Full tactical treatment of these in
1v1s and through balls lives in [one-v-one & breakaway](one-v-one-breakaway.md);
the angle logic lives in [positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md).

## Hold-and-React — The Movement Discipline

Footwork is not only about moving; it is about knowing when to **stop**
moving. All three sources converge here, and it is the discipline that
binds the stance, the set, and the first step together.

- **Robo:** "Anticipating what might happen, before it happens, usually
  leads to failure; holding a position and then reacting usually gives the
  goalkeeper a good opportunity to react positively." Just prior to a
  shooting situation, "the only response is to stand up, hold a position,
  and react quickly when the ball is played." The goalkeeper "needs to
  stand up, without anticipating, when the ball is hit on goal."
- **ECNL:** the proactive ideal — eliminate danger *before* it happens
  through positioning and angle-cutting (Stalk/Steal Ground), so that by
  the moment of the shot the keeper is already set and merely has to react.

These are not in tension: you are proactive with your **movement** (taking
ground, cutting angle, getting set early) and patient with your
**reaction** (holding the set position, not lunging before the ball is
struck). The footwork goal is to arrive set, early, and still — then react.

## Footwork as a Training Staple

ECNL and Plan both list **Footwork first in every age band** (U9–U12,
U13–U15, U16–U18 in Plan) and prescribe that warm-ups "use the technique
basics as a staple item." Footwork is trained every session, at every age,
as the foundation the rest of the session is built on. Plan also lists
**Back to Bar** as a recurring positioning/footwork item from U13 up
(footwork relative to the goal frame). For how this slots into a session,
see the [training methodology](../framework/training-methodology.md) and
the age-banded [development framework](../framework/development-framework.md).

## Quick Reference — Cues

| Cue | Source | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| First step by the foot closest to the ball | Robo | The near foot leads; the far foot trails and follows smoothly. |
| First step as long as possible, adjust to the serve | Robo | Maximize and angle the first step to the actual service. |
| Lead away (with the near-side foot) | Robo | Push off the foot on the side the ball is shot to. |
| Square to the line of flight | Robo | Keep the body facing the ball's path. |
| Power Step | ECNL / Plan | Near foot steps laterally toward the ball; bridges into the dive. |
| Goalie Stance / Ready Position | ECNL / Plan | Hands at sides, knees bent, squat, feet shoulder-width, eyes on ball. |
| Set Yourself | ECNL / Plan | Stop moving, get into Ready Position before the shot. |
| Step sideways out of the sprint | Robo | Recovery step that kills momentum and gets you set. |
| Stalk | ECNL / Plan | Keep feet in a LOW Ready Position while tracking a live dribbler. |
| Steal Ground | ECNL / Plan | Step toward the dribbler on the touch (ball off the foot) to cut angle. |
| Hold, don't anticipate | Robo | Stand up and react to the struck ball — don't guess early. |
