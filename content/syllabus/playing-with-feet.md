# Playing With Feet — Footskills, Receiving, Build-Out

The modern goalkeeper is the deepest outfield player as often as the
last line of defense. This category covers the keeper-with-the-ball-at-feet:
receiving back-passes, taking a first touch away from pressure, building
out of the back, switching the point of attack, and clearing under
pressure. It is distinct from hand-distribution (punts, dropkicks,
throws) — for those, see [distribution](distribution.md). Footwork *as
movement around the goal* (shuffling, stalking, the power step) lives in
[footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md); this file is about feet *on
the ball*.

A faithfulness note up front: of the three sources, the deepest technical
detail on kicking a moving ball off the ground is in **Robo**; **Plan**
makes "Footskills" / "Distribution" / "Back to Bar" explicit syllabus
items by age; **ECNL/Plan** supply the build-out and switch-the-play
*vocabulary*. None of the three gives a granular "first touch away from
pressure" technique breakdown — that idea is carried implicitly by the
back-pass cues (drive away vs. cushion) and by the communication terms
that tell teammates when the keeper has time. Where a point comes from
only one source, it is flagged.

---

## Why this category exists: the back-pass rule context

The keeper plays with feet because the Laws force it. A goalkeeper may
**not** handle the ball inside their own box in four situations — and
committing one of them inside the box concedes an **indirect free kick**
to the opposition from the spot of the offence (Plan + ECNL, "Indirect
Free Kick inside the Box"):

1. Controlling the ball with the hands for **more than six seconds**.
2. Touching the ball again with the hands after releasing it from
   possession and before another player has touched it (the
   **double-touch**).
3. Touching the ball with the hands after it was **deliberately kicked
   to them by a team-mate** (the deliberate **back-pass**).
4. Touching the ball with the hands after **receiving it directly from a
   throw-in by a team-mate**.

Items 3 and 4 are the load-bearing ones for this category: a deliberate
back-pass (kicked) or a team-mate's throw-in **cannot** be picked up. The
keeper must therefore be a competent foot-player. This is not optional
polish — it is a rule-enforced requirement of the position.

> Per Robo, the back-pass cues are "likely the most familiar to the team
> coach of any technique in the document," because playing a moving ball
> off the ground is ordinary outfield technique. The keeper just has to
> own it under goal-line pressure.

For how to defend the indirect free kick *if you concede one*, see the
[drill library](../drills/library.md) and the kill-the-play rule below.

## Receiving the back-pass

**Hold to read the pass, then commit (Robo).** As the ball is played
back, the keeper holds position to assess two things: the **pressure on
the back-pass** (is an opponent chasing it?) and the **quality of the
pass** (pace, bounce, accuracy). Holding lets the keeper make minor
adjustments as the ball arrives.

**Timing against pressure (Robo):**

- **Meet the ball early** if the back-pass is being contested by an
  opponent. The longer the keeper waits, the more time the chaser has to
  close.
- If there is no pressure, holding briefly is fine — it buys the
  adjustment window above and lets the keeper pick a better next action.

**First-touch / striking surface by ball type (Robo):**

- **Smooth back-passes** (ball arriving cleanly on the ground) can be
  played with the **instep** and **driven away** — a clean first contact
  that moves the ball out of the danger zone or into the next pass.
- **Balls that come in on the ground and bounce** might play best with
  the **side of the foot** — more surface, more control on an awkward
  bounce.

This is the closest the sources come to "first touch away from pressure":
the instep-drive moves the ball away under contest; the side-foot
cushions an unpredictable bounce. Robo does not separately catalog a
turn-and-go or a directional-touch technique — extending beyond
drive-vs-cushion would be inventing detail the source does not contain.

## The build-out: keeper as the start of possession

Both ECNL and Plan frame the keeper as the **organizer and origin** of
playing out of the back, expressed through the communication vocabulary
rather than through a step-by-step build-out shape (the sources are
reference handbooks, not tactical playbooks). The relevant calls:

| Call | Meaning | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **(KEEPER'S) HOME** | Tells a teammate to play the ball **back to the goalkeeper** — invites a back-pass to start build-up. | ECNL, Plan |
| **TIME** | Tells a teammate there is **little pressure** on the ball and they have time to **play it with their feet**. | ECNL, Plan |
| **TURN (LEFT/RIGHT)** | Lets a teammate know they have time to **turn with the ball**; the direction tells them which way to face. | ECNL, Plan |
| **OPEN UP** | Tells a teammate to take an **open body position** to see as much of the field as possible; used when the ball is played wide. | ECNL, Plan |
| **MAN ON** | Warns a teammate they are (or are about to be) **under pressure** — emphasize urgency. | ECNL, Plan |

The principle behind all of these (ECNL/Plan): **"A goalkeeper is never a
cheerleader."** Communication is command, not noise — loud, clear,
concise, and tactically useful. In build-out, the keeper's voice is what
turns a back-pass into the first phase of an attack: HOME to invite it,
TIME/TURN/OPEN UP to free the receiver, MAN ON to abort. See the
[communication syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md) for the
full four-stage command curriculum.

## Switching play

When the build-out needs to escape pressure on one side, the keeper
moves the ball — or directs it — to the other (ECNL "Distribution /
Playing With Feet"; Plan Stage Four):

- **SWITCH** — quickly send the ball to a player on the **other side of
  the field**.
- **SWING** — drop the ball to a player so they may send it across, and
  **tell the opposite-side player to get wide** so the switch has a
  target.
- **SHIFT (RIGHT/LEFT)** — moves the whole team's defensive shape toward
  the ball after the opposition swings the point of attack (the defensive
  mirror of the keeper's own switch).

SWITCH/SWING can be executed by the keeper's own foot-pass or commanded
to a teammate; the sources treat them as commands in the keeper's
vocabulary, not as a prescribed kicking technique. For the long-range
foot or hand-pass that *executes* a switch, see
[distribution](distribution.md).

## Dealing with pressure: the sweeper-keeper and the clearance

The keeper playing out is, by definition, playing under the threat of
the press. Two rules govern what happens when build-out is not on.

**1. Clear it when you cannot play out — INTO TOUCH (ECNL/Plan).**
When the defense is **not in position to build a counter or play out of
the back**, the keeper uses **INTO TOUCH**: express urgency, clear the
ball out of bounds, then follow with instructions to the entire defense.
This is the disciplined version of Robo's blunter rule of thumb below.

**2. "When in doubt, kick it out" (Robo).** Robo's guiding rule for the
*developing* keeper: if the read is unclear, get rid of it. The maturity
arc is explicit — "as the GK matures he/she will have to develop sound
passing techniques from deep positions on the field." So the rule is a
floor, not a ceiling: clear safely now, build the deep passing range over
years.

**The sweeper-keeper read (ECNL "Through Balls").** The modern keeper
also plays with feet *outside* the box as the last sweeper behind a high
line. On a ball played through the defense, the keeper must **read the
ball and decide**: can I **step and smother**, or should I **steal ground
and stalk** and prepare for a shot or 1v1? Once the keeper decides to go
out, **they have committed and must stick to the decision.** Awareness of
**backside opponents finding the seams** is part of the read. The
footwork mechanics of this advance (stalk, steal ground, the committed
sprint) are detailed in [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md) and the
decision-making in [one-v-one-breakaway](one-v-one-breakaway.md) — here
the point is that sweeping is a *feet* action, and a clearance under
pressure with the foot is the same skill as the back-pass clearance.

## Killing the play on a conceded indirect free kick

The flip side of the back-pass rule: if the keeper **concedes** an
indirect free kick inside the box (by committing one of the four offences
above), both ECNL and Plan give the same instruction — **the keeper must
KILL THE PLAY**: kick the ball **as far as they can** to force a whistle,
**even if it draws a yellow card.** ("If it draws a yellow card, so be
it." — Plan.) The keeper's feet here are a clock-killing, restart-forcing
tool. Defending the indirect kick once it is set (the wall covering as
much goal as possible, the charge to cut the angle) is covered in
[positioning-angles](positioning-angles.md) and the
[drill library](../drills/library.md).

## Failure terms to know

Two outcomes name the ways a keeper gets beaten — useful shorthand in
coaching feedback (ECNL/Plan general glossary; see also
[vocabulary](../vocabulary.md)):

- **Chip** — the ball goes **over the keeper's head and into the goal.**
  In the breakaway/sweeper context this is "the most important worry in
  the beginning": stepping out to play with feet must never expose the
  space behind for a chip.
- **Meg** (nutmeg) — the ball goes **in-between the legs.** A foot-skill
  cautionary tale: poor stance or a heavy touch lets the ball through the
  legs.

## Where the sources agree, differ, and go silent

- **Agreement:** all three treat playing-with-feet as a real,
  rule-driven part of the position. ECNL and Plan share an identical
  build-out / switch / clear vocabulary (HOME, TIME, OPEN UP, SWITCH,
  SWING, INTO TOUCH) and an identical indirect-FK kill-the-play rule.
- **Robo is the technique source.** Only Robo breaks down *how* to strike
  a moving back-pass (hold-to-read, meet-early-under-pressure, instep to
  drive vs. side-of-foot for a bounce) and only Robo states the
  "when in doubt, kick it out" maturity arc toward deep-position passing.
- **Plan is the syllabus source.** Only Plan makes the progression
  explicit by age: **Footskills** is a U9–U12 item; **Distribution**
  appears at U13–U15 and **Advanced Distribution** at U16–U18; **Back to
  Bar** runs from U13 up. (Robo lists distribution among its ten
  techniques but does not age-band footskills specifically.)
- **Silence:** none of the three gives a detailed "first touch away from
  pressure" / receive-and-turn technique, a pressing-trap escape pattern,
  or goalkeeper passing-range drills with distances. Those are gaps in
  the source material, not omissions here. The development framework
  ([../framework/development-framework.md]) should treat deep passing
  range as a multi-year skill per Robo, consistent with his note that
  distribution techniques "take several years and many matches to get a
  good feel for."

## Sources

- **Plan** — *Goalkeeper Plan 2015-2016.* Primary for this category:
  Footskills (U9–U12), Distribution / Advanced Distribution (U13–U18),
  Back to Bar (U13+) as explicit age-banded syllabus items; the full
  build-out / switch / clear command glossary (HOME, TIME, OPEN UP,
  SWITCH, SWING, INTO TOUCH, MAN ON, TURN, SHIFT); the indirect-free-kick
  offences and kill-the-play rule.
- **ECNL** — *ECNL Goalkeeping Curriculum & Handbook, First Edition*
  (Birmingham United Soccer Association / BUSA). The "Distribution /
  Playing With Feet" framing, the same command vocabulary, the
  proactive/sweeper-keeper philosophy ("eliminate the danger before it
  happens"), the Through-Balls read (step-and-smother vs.
  steal-ground-and-stalk; commit and stick), and the Chip/Meg failure
  terms.
- **Robo** — *Developing a Training Program for Coaching Goalkeeping*
  (Rob Walker, US National Goalkeeping Staff). The technical detail on
  kicking a moving ball off the ground (the back-pass): hold to assess
  pressure and pass quality, meet early when contested, instep to drive
  smooth balls away vs. side of foot for bouncing balls; the "when in
  doubt, kick it out" rule of thumb and the long-term arc toward sound
  deep-position passing.
