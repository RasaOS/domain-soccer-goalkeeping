# Positioning & Angles

Positioning is the goalkeeper's invisible save — the work done before the
ball is struck that decides whether a save is even required. Robo frames it
as the "Qualities of Positional Play": a set of considerations and cues that
govern where the keeper starts, how the keeper moves as the ball moves, and
how the keeper reads the moment of the shot. ECNL and the Plan add the
decision-making frame (proactive, not reactive) and the movement tools
(stalking, stealing ground, sweeping) that turn position into action.

The through-line across all three docs: **hold position and react, do not
anticipate.** Anticipating what might happen before it happens usually leads
to failure (Robo). The keeper eliminates danger before it happens through
*position*, not through guessing the shot (ECNL).

This file covers angle play, depth and starting position, near-post
priority, sweeping behind a high line, and the "stand up and react" reading
discipline. For the footwork that delivers the keeper to these positions see
[footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md); for the dives that finish the save
see [shot-stopping-diving](shot-stopping-diving.md); for cross/corner
positioning see [aerial-crosses](aerial-crosses.md); for advancing onto
through balls and breakaways see [one-v-one-breakaway](one-v-one-breakaway.md).

## The six positioning considerations (Robo)

Robo's "Qualities of Positional Play" is a 6-item list, each expanded in
detail. These are the spine of the category:

1. **Final-third starting position** — when the ball is in the final third,
   the starting position is **1–2 yards off the end line**.
2. **Move to protect the near/far post** — as the ball moves, the keeper
   must move to protect the near post and then the far post.
3. **Stand up, without anticipating** — when the ball is hit on goal, the
   keeper needs to stand up, without anticipating.
4. **"Can I win the ball?"** — when coming off the line, the keeper must
   think "can I win the ball?"
5. **Catch, parry or punch** — should the ball be caught, parried or
   punched?
6. **(The reading discipline that binds them)** — read the play and respond;
   hold a position and react rather than anticipate.

The 17 cues below are the detailed coaching points that sit underneath these
six considerations.

## Depth and starting position

**Starting position is a place to begin, not a fixed spot** (Robo). It is the
reference point the keeper adjusts from as the ball moves.

- **Final third:** start **1–2 yards off the end line** (Robo). This is the
  default depth when the opposition has the ball in the attacking third.
- As the opposition plays in the final third, the keeper recognizes **two
  situations simultaneously** (Robo):
  1. *Can I move forward off the starting position to narrow the angle?* —
     getting **set 4, 5 or 6 yards from goal**.
  2. *Could the serve be a drive, volley or chip to the crossbar?* — good
     keepers **always consider how far they are from the crossbar and how
     long it will take to recover to defend it.**
- The depth decision is therefore a constant trade: step forward to cut the
  angle of a near shot, but never so far that a ball lifted to the crossbar
  cannot be recovered. The two priorities are weighed against each other on
  every repositioning.

The Plan lists **"Back to Bar"** as a recurring positioning/footwork syllabus
item from U13 up — the discipline of knowing where the goal frame is behind
you so you can recover to the line and protect the crossbar. (The Plan names
it as a training category; it does not expand the mechanics in prose.)

## Angle play

The keeper's lateral position relative to the ball determines how much goal
is covered. Robo's rule of thumb:

- **For a shot hit in the central area, the keeper's position should be
  central.** For a ball from the right, the position should be **tempered to
  the right** (Robo). The keeper shades toward the side the ball is on,
  reducing the open angle on the near side without abandoning the far side.

### The ending-angle process (Robo)

How the keeper moves to keep the correct angle as the ball moves:

- **Ball played straight toward goal** → come **straight off the line**.
- **Ball moves "square" across the field** → **move across the goal**
  similarly (matching the ball's lateral travel).
- **Ball played toward goal but at an angle away** → move to the new angle by
  going **across AND up at an angle** to narrow the angle.

The third case is the subtle one: a ball traveling diagonally toward goal
requires a diagonal recovery — both sideways and forward — so the keeper
arrives at the narrowed angle set and balanced rather than drifting flat
across the line.

### Angle tools: stalk and steal ground (ECNL, Plan)

ECNL and the Plan supply the *movement* vocabulary for cutting angles
on a live, dribbling attacker:

- **Steal Ground** — while an opponent is dribbling, the keeper steps toward
  them, **taking ground while the ball is away from the feet.** This lets the
  keeper **cut angles and shorten the distance to the ball.** (ECNL, Plan.)
- **Stalk** — in a LOW Ready Position, the keeper keeps their feet while
  following the opponent with the ball; requires better-than-average
  footwork to maintain a low center of gravity and steady balance during
  movement (ECNL, Plan).

The timing rule is critical and appears in both ECNL and the Plan: **steal
ground forward only once the opponent dribbles the ball away from their
foot** — never while the ball is at the attacker's feet, where they can shoot
or push past you. See [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md) for the full
stance/stalk/steal-ground/power-step sequence.

## Near-post priority

All three docs agree the near post comes first. Robo states it most
directly:

- **Coverage of the near post is the first priority.** (Robo)
- **The second priority — always balanced with the first — is to create the
  right kind of angle to get across to the far post.** (Robo)

The near post is the shorter, more direct path to goal and the harder shot to
recover; it is conceded only by gross mispositioning, which is why it is
covered first. But near-post coverage is never absolute: it is *balanced*
against keeping enough angle to travel to the far post. A keeper glued to the
near post who cannot reach a far-post finish has solved the wrong problem.

ECNL extends near-post priority to the **wall on free kicks**: *remember, the
wall doesn't stop people from scoring near post — the keeper must plan to dive
that way to cover that area.* People love to shoot over the wall, so the
wall's job is to make the shot difficult, not to cover the near post; the
keeper covers the near post themselves. See [aerial-crosses](aerial-crosses.md)
for full set-piece/wall positioning.

## Stand up and react — the reading discipline

This is the philosophical center of the category, stated most fully by Robo
and echoed by ECNL.

- **Read the play and respond.** Just prior to a shooting situation, the only
  response is to **stand up, hold a position, and react quickly when the ball
  is played.** (Robo)
- **Anticipating what might happen, before it happens, usually leads to
  failure.** Holding a position and then reacting usually gives the keeper a
  good opportunity to react positively. (Robo)
- The mark of a mature keeper: they **appear calm just as the ball is hit.**
  Asked what they think about when set and holding, a developed keeper
  answers **"absolutely nothing."** (Robo)
- **Developing the ability to hold and then react takes years of practice.**
  (Robo) This is a long-term development item, not a quick fix.

ECNL frames the same idea as *proactive vs. reactive* and locates it in
position rather than in the dive: **"You eliminate the danger before it
happens. If you only react, then you have to make a save."** Note this is not
a contradiction of Robo's "react, don't anticipate" — ECNL's point is that
correct *positioning* (proactive) removes the need for the reactive scramble,
while Robo's point is that at the *moment of the shot* the keeper holds and
reacts rather than guessing the direction. Both reject the dive-early gamble.

ECNL also names the broader truth: **decision-making is the hardest thing to
teach** — "to have the right position, to know when to go or not to go, where
to move, not to move" — and it is learned throughout a keeper's career.

### Ready stances by distance (Robo)

The body posture that supports "hold and react" changes with how close the
threat is:

- **Ball away from goal (16–24 yards):** be **upright, shoulders rolled
  forward, hands hanging downward and slightly tense (not stiff), feet spread
  about shoulder width, weight balanced on the "ball" of each foot.**
- **Ball/attacker close to goal (3, 4 or 5 yards), or the keeper has come off
  the line for a through ball:** **hold with the knees slightly bent and the
  hands come down low to protect a shot from going under the body.**

ECNL/Plan's **Goalie Stance / Ready Position** (two hands down to the sides,
knees bent, body in a squat, feet shoulder width, no movement — just
attention to the ball) and **Set Yourself** (stop moving and get into Ready
Position before the shot) describe the same set discipline in their
vocabulary. See [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md) and the
[glossary](../vocabulary.md).

## Sweeping behind a high line — through balls

When the defense plays a high line, the space behind it becomes the keeper's
to defend. ECNL and the Plan cover this as **Through Balls**; Robo covers the
same decision under "can I win the ball?"

**The read (ECNL, Plan):** the keeper must read the ball and decide whether
they can **win it**, OR **steal ground and stalk.** On a ball played through
the defense the keeper must decide whether they can **step and smother**, or
**steal ground / stalk and prepare for a shot or 1v1.**

**Awareness (ECNL, Plan):** be aware of **backside opponents looking to make
runs through the defense** — find the seams the ball can get through.

**Commit and stay (ECNL, Plan):** **if the keeper decides to go out, they have
now committed and must stick to their decision.** The half-committed sweep —
starting to go, then hesitating — is the failure mode. Decide, then execute.

### "Can I win the ball?" — a consideration, not a commandment (Robo)

Robo's framing of the same decision is the most detailed. When a through ball
or cross is delivered, the keeper runs through a thought process — it is **a
consideration, not a commandment**:

- *Do I hold?*
- *Do I come part way and take a new angle and hold?*
- *Or can I come all the way and win the ball outright?*

**The three factors in "can I win the ball?" (Robo):**

1. **Total distance to travel** to win it.
2. **Can a teammate win it, or is a teammate closer?**
3. **Are there obstacles** (opponents and teammates) impeding the path?

ECNL/Plan reinforce that the keeper's **true job on breakaways is to delay and
allow teammates to recover and help** — winning the ball outright is one
option among several, not the default. Keep the feet as long as possible. The
full breakaway/sweeping mechanics live in
[one-v-one-breakaway](one-v-one-breakaway.md).

### KEEPER! — committing verbally (Robo, ECNL, Plan)

When the keeper decides to come and win a ball, the verbal response is
**"KEEPER!"** (Robo). This is a command of **courtesy and tactics**:

- It tells **everyone the decision is made.**
- **Teammates take up positions of cover** behind the advancing keeper.
- **Opponents know a physical challenge is coming** for the ball.

ECNL/Plan add the mirror call **"AWAY!"** for when the keeper *cannot* make
the play and wants a teammate to deal with it, and the rule that the keeper
must claim or release **every** ball — KEEPER or AWAY, **no matter what, every
single time.** KEEPER is always two syllables — never allow the keeper to
simply yell "Keep." See [communication-syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md)
and [psychology-mentality](psychology-mentality.md).

## Catch, parry or punch — the positional ramification

The handling decision is mostly technical (can the ball be caught?), but Robo
notes it has **positional ramifications**:

- **A ball that is held is the safest choice** a keeper can make — it ends the
  threat.
- **A ball punched or parried can keep a team under continuous pressure** —
  the ball is still live, and the keeper's position must immediately account
  for the rebound and the recovery.
- **Train to catch first, and parry or punch out of danger as a second and
  last resort.**

This is why positioning and handling are not separable: where the keeper is
*and* whether they catch determine whether one shot becomes a sequence of
shots. See [handling-catching](handling-catching.md).

## Where this sits in the age-band syllabus

The Plan lists **Positioning/Angles** as an explicit syllabus item in **every
age band**, escalating in sophistication:

| Age band | Item |
|---|---|
| **U9–U12** | Positioning/Angles |
| **U13–U15** | Positioning/Angles (+ Back to Bar, Communication, Set Plays) |
| **U16–U18** | **Advanced** Positioning/Angles (+ Back to Bar, Advanced Communication, Advanced Set Plays) |

Robo's age framing reinforces *when* positioning gets weight: **younger
keepers (U-12 through U-14) are best served with an emphasis on technique**;
**older keepers (U-17 through U-19) really need to focus on technique,
positioning AND fitness.** Positioning is layered on once the technical
foundation is in place — it is the work of the maturing keeper. The full
age-band progression lives in
[development-framework](../framework/development-framework.md).

## Coaching it

Both Robo and the Plan note that holding-and-reacting **takes years of
practice** — positioning is trained over a long horizon, not grooved in a
session. Use the [drill library](../drills/library.md) for the
stalk / steal-ground / through-ball-decision activities (the Plan provides
diagram-based Through-Ball Decision and Breakaway Decision activities). Robo's
training methodology (Simple-to-Complex and Whole-Part-Whole) applies: build
the positioning read from parts, then return it to the most realistic
match-condition replication — live service with attackers — because position
and decision-making only hold up under real pressure. See
[training-methodology](../framework/training-methodology.md).
