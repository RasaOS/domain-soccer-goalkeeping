# Handling / Catching

Catching is the foundation of the position. Curriculum should be built
around goalkeeping essentials — **catching, footwork, and positioning** —
and from that foundation competent goalkeeping is attainable in the short
term while world-class goalkeeping is a long-term build (Robo). This file
covers secure handling: the shape of the hands, the two organizing cases
of **ball-at-body** and **ball-away-from-body**, the low / mid / high
ranges, and the named handling techniques (Basket Catch, Scoop, Contour
Catch, Three Hands). Diving saves of balls fully beyond reach live in
[shot-stopping-diving](shot-stopping-diving.md); aerial service and crosses
have their own file, [aerial-crosses](aerial-crosses.md), and are cross-
referenced where the catch itself is shared.

The three source docs converge strongly here. Robo is the richest on the
*mechanics and decision* of the catch (hand shape, first-step-to-the-ball,
locking into the chest, lateral footwork). ECNL and Plan supply the named
**technique vocabulary** (Basket Catch / Scoop / Contour Catch / Three
Hands) and the failure terms (Chip, Meg). Where a point comes from only
one source, it is marked.

---

## First principles of catching (Robo)

These are Robo's "Qualities" of catching — the first line of training for
a developing goalkeeper. They govern every catch in this file:

- **The hands are the first part of the goalkeeper's body to touch the
  ball.** Hands lead; the body follows in behind.
- **The ball ends up curled or tucked into the chest.** A catch is not
  finished until the ball is secured to the body.
- **Good catching does not allow for second finishing opportunities.** A
  clean catch denies the rebound. This is the standard.
- **The hallmark of good catching comes from good lateral footwork.** Most
  catching failures are footwork failures — the keeper didn't get the body
  behind the ball. See [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md).
- **Catching with two hands is preferable to parrying or punching with
  one.** Catch first; parry or punch only as a last resort (this echoes
  Robo's through-line: "catch first, parry or punch only as a last
  resort").
- **Good catchers get as much body behind each catch as possible.** The
  body is the backstop behind the hands.

A decision-making note that frames the whole category (Robo): hold position
and **react** rather than anticipate — "anticipating what might happen,
before it happens usually leads to failure." The catch is a reaction
executed from a balanced set position, not a guess.

## The set / ready position

Before any catch, the keeper is **set**. ECNL and Plan describe the
ready position identically:

> Goalie Stance / Ready Position — two hands down to the sides of the body,
> knees bent, body in a squat position, feet shoulder width apart, no
> movement — just attention to the ball.

From here the hands travel to the ball and the feet drive the body in
behind it. (Both ECNL and Plan; the set position is also the launch point
for footwork and diving — see [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md).)

## Hand shape

The shape of the hands is the single biggest technical concern on a ball
at the body (Robo). Two source vocabularies describe the same thing from
two angles — use them together:

- **Concave hand position (Robo).** The fingers and thumb of each hand
  form a **concave (cupped) position behind the ball**. Hands point
  **downward on low balls** and **upward on higher balls**. The cup of
  the hands is *behind* the ball, never on the side of it.
- **Contour Catch (ECNL / Plan).** Two hands **behind** the ball, forming
  a **diamond with the index fingers and thumbs**. This is the named
  technique for the same shaping — the diamond places both hands behind
  the ball so it cannot squeeze through.

**The most common developing-keeper error (Robo):** young goalkeepers
struggle with driven balls — on the ground or in the air — because their
**hands are on the side of the ball and not behind it.** Driven service is
the test of correct hand shape. Drill the hands *behind* the ball, in the
concave / diamond shape, until it is automatic.

See the [glossary](../vocabulary.md) for *concave hand position* and
*Contour Catch*.

## Ball at the body

The ball-at-body catch is the bread-and-butter save: the ball comes
straight at or near the keeper, who gets the body squarely behind it.

**Mechanics (Robo):**

1. **Shape the hands** in the concave / diamond position behind the ball —
   pointed **down for low balls**, **up for higher balls**.
2. **On low balls, get the hands near the ground.** Robo flags a body-type
   reality: long legs can make it hard to get a bent posture behind the
   ball. The keeper must still bend and get a barrier behind the line of
   the ball.
3. **On contact, curl the ball into the chest.** The ball does not stop at
   the hands — it is brought in and secured.
4. **A ball played into the chest can be absorbed by taking a little jump
   forward** as the ball is pulled into the chest. The forward motion
   absorbs pace.
5. **After bringing the ball into the body, move forward with the ball.**
   This footwork is **absolutely critical in "killing" the momentum of the
   shot** — finishing the catch with several forward steps **"locks" the
   ball into the chest.** (See *lock the ball* in the
   [glossary](../vocabulary.md).)

**Named techniques for ball-at-body (ECNL / Plan):**

- **Basket Catch** — create a **basket** with your hands and arms to
  securely catch the ball, **using your torso as a barrier.** This is the
  torso-supported catch at chest/midsection height — the same "curl into
  the chest" Robo describes, named.
- **Scoop** — a Basket Catch performed on a **rolling ground ball**:
  scoop the ball from a roll on the ground, **allowing it to roll up and
  into your arms.** This is the low-ball, ball-at-body case for ground
  service.

## Ball away from the body

When the ball is to the side — reachable on the feet but not in the
keeper's current line — the catch becomes a **footwork problem first**.
Robo is emphatic and specific here:

- **The first step to the ball is the most critical factor.** Push off
  with the **foot closest to the ball.** If the shot is to the keeper's
  right, **"lead away" with the right foot** (see *lead away* in the
  [glossary](../vocabulary.md)).
- **If more than one step is needed, take additional steps to get the body
  behind the ball.** The goal is always to convert a ball-away-from-body
  into a ball-at-body by moving the body into line.
- **Finishing the catch with several steps after the ball comes in helps
  "lock" the ball into the chest** — the same lock-the-ball finish as the
  ball-at-body catch.

This is the lateral-footwork skill Robo names as the *hallmark* of good
catching. The technique of moving across the goal (shuffling vs. sprinting)
belongs to [footwork-movement](footwork-movement.md); the **catch at the
end of the movement** is governed by the same hand shape and
lock-the-ball finish as above.

**Decision boundary:** the ball-away-from-body catch is for a ball the
keeper can still get the body behind on the feet. A ball served stiffly
just to the side, beyond a step, becomes a **collapse dive** — the foot
closest to the serve slides away and collapses while the hands push to
the ball (Robo). That technique is covered in
[shot-stopping-diving](shot-stopping-diving.md).

## Low / mid / high ranges

The same catch is re-shaped by the height of the ball:

| Range | Hand shape | Primary technique | Source notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low / ground** | Hands point **down**, near the ground | **Scoop** (rolling ball) → **Three Hands** (handling to the ground) | Robo: long legs make the low bent posture hard — bend anyway. |
| **Mid / body** | Concave/diamond, behind the ball | **Basket Catch** / **Contour Catch**, curl into chest | Robo: little jump forward absorbs pace into the chest. |
| **High (overhead, served from distance)** | Hands point **up**, behind the ball | Catch at a **high point out in front of the body**, one-footed takeoff | Cross-ref [aerial-crosses](aerial-crosses.md). |

### Three Hands (handling to the ground — ECNL / Plan)

For low service handled all the way to the ground, ECNL and Plan name the
**Three Hands** system: two hands plus the ground create a three-hand
structure around the ball — **one hand on top, one hand behind, while the
ground becomes the third hand under the ball.** This is the secure way to
collect a ball onto the ground (e.g., smothering at the feet of an
attacker or settling a low driven ball you choose not to bring to the
chest). The ground takes the place of the third hand the keeper does not
have.

### High balls

The full high-ball / aerial technique — hold position to assess speed,
spin and trajectory; path to the ball in line; **one-footed takeoff to
catch at a high point out in front of the body**; bend at the waist and
move forward to lock the ball in — is detailed in
[aerial-crosses](aerial-crosses.md). It is noted here only because the
**hand shape (concave, pointed up) and the lock-the-ball finish are the
same** as the body catches in this file; the high ball is the catch plus
a jump.

## Failure terms (ECNL / Plan)

Two named failures that the catch exists to prevent — keep them in the
shared vocabulary so coaches and keepers name errors consistently:

- **Chip** — the ball goes **over the GK's head** and into the goal.
- **Meg** (nutmeg) — the ball goes **in-between the legs.**

Both are handling failures of position and barrier: the Chip is a
positioning / aerial-judgment failure (see
[positioning-angles](positioning-angles.md)); the Meg is a failure to get
the body and hands behind a low ball — exactly the "hands on the side of
the ball, not behind it" error above.

## Claiming the catch verbally

Every catch the keeper can make is claimed out loud. ECNL and Plan both
lock this:

- **"KEEPER"** — used **every time** the keeper can make a save or handle
  the ball. **Always two syllables — never allow the keeper to simply yell
  "Keep" (one syllable).** The call claims the ball for the keeper and
  signals control to both teammates and opponents.
- **"AWAY"** — used when the keeper **cannot** make the save or wants a
  teammate to control the ball.

The rule across set pieces and traffic: call **"KEEPER!" or "AWAY!" every
single time** (ECNL / Plan). Full communication syllabus in
[../framework/communication-syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md).

## Age-band emphasis

Both age-graded sources put **catching among the very first technical
priorities** and weight it by age:

- **Plan** lists **Catching** in the U9–U12 and U13–U15 syllabi as a core
  technical block, graduating to **"Advanced Handling/Catching"** at
  U16–U18.
- **Robo** frames younger keepers (U-12 through U-14) as **best served by
  an emphasis on technique** — train once or twice a week, ~45–60 min,
  technique over positioning/fitness. Older keepers (U-17 to U-19) add
  positioning and fitness on top of continued technical work.

Translation for the catch specifically: **shape and barrier first**
(concave hands behind the ball, body behind the hands, curl to the chest)
for the youngest keepers, with **footwork-driven ball-away-from-body** and
**Three Hands / Scoop ground handling** layered in as the keeper matures.
See [../framework/development-framework](../framework/development-framework.md)
for the full age model.

## Drills

Handling is trained directly by the named techniques, which double as
drills in Plan's library. See the [drill library](../drills/library.md)
for full write-ups; the handling-relevant entries are:

- **Basket Catch** — *objective:* securely catch the ball into the body.
  Create a basket with hands and arms, using the torso as a barrier.
- **Scoop** — *objective:* securely collect a rolling ground ball. Basket
  Catch a ball rolling on the ground, letting it roll up into the arms.
- **Contour Catch** — *objective:* catch with hands correctly shaped
  behind the ball. Two hands behind the ball, forming a diamond with the
  index fingers and thumbs.
- **Three Hands** — *objective:* secure the ball to the ground with a safe
  hand structure. One hand on top, one hand behind, ground as the third
  hand under the ball.

Coaching emphasis when running these (from Robo's qualities): keep the
hands **behind** the ball (not on the side), **curl every catch into the
chest**, and **finish with forward footwork to lock the ball in.** Build
from ball-at-body to ball-away-from-body so the keeper's **first step to
the ball** and **lateral footwork** are trained as part of the catch, not
separately. Plan's two delivery methods — **Simple-to-Complex** and
**Whole-Part-Whole** — both end by returning the keeper to a realistic,
match-condition repetition of the catch; see
[../framework/training-methodology](../framework/training-methodology.md).
