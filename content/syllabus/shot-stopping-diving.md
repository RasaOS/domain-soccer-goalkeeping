# Shot-Stopping & Diving

Shot-stopping is the act of getting hands and body behind a struck ball and
keeping it out of the net. Diving is how the goalkeeper extends that reach
when the ball is served such that staying upright would leave the ball
unabsorbed. This file covers the dive techniques themselves; the readiness
and movement that precede them live in [footwork & movement](footwork-movement.md),
and what the goalkeeper does with the ball once it's reached — the catch —
lives in [handling & catching](handling-catching.md).

All three source documents treat diving as a graded family of techniques,
selected by **how far the ball is from the body**. Two of them (Plan, ECNL)
name a clean three-tier ladder; the third (Robo) gives the deepest mechanical
detail, organized as near-body vs. well-away-from-body. The two framings
agree and reinforce each other, and are mapped together below.

## The decision to dive

Before technique comes the read. Per Robo, the diving decision is a question:
**has the ball been served such that staying upright would result in the ball
not being absorbed?** If yes, diving lets the goalkeeper extend the hands and
body behind the ball. If the ball can be reached from the feet, stay up and
get the body behind it — diving is not the default, it is the response to a
ball you cannot otherwise cover.

Both Plan and ECNL frame the moment before the shot the same way: **Set
Yourself** — stop moving and get into the Ready Position before the shot is
struck (see [footwork & movement](footwork-movement.md) for the Ready Position
and the [glossary](../vocabulary.md)). A still, balanced, low base is what
makes a clean push-off possible. Robo states the same idea positionally:
just prior to a shooting situation the only response is to **stand up, hold a
position, and react quickly** when the ball is played — "anticipating what
might happen, before it happens usually leads to failure" (see
[positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md)).

## The power step — the launch of every dive

Plan and ECNL both anchor diving on a single shared mechanic, the **Power
Step**: when a shot has been taken, **the foot closest to the ball steps
laterally toward the ball.** This step is used in the process of every dive —
it is the spring source for the longest save (the Extension/Power Dive) and is
present in shorter form on the others. The principle "push off the foot
nearest/closest to the ball" recurs across all three documents and is the
single most important footwork cue in this category.

## The three-tier ladder (Plan + ECNL)

Plan and ECNL grade diving into three techniques by save distance. The
terminology and definitions below are verbatim-faithful to both documents
(they agree word-for-word):

| Tier | Technique | Save distance | Mechanic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Short | **Collapse Dive** | near the body | Take the closest leg to the ball out from under yourself to fall to the ground |
| Medium | **Step Dive** | medium | Take an appropriate step with the foot closest to the ball to fall to the ground |
| Long | **Extension / Power Dive** | far / full stretch | Using the Power Step, the goalkeeper springs toward the ball |

This ladder maps the difficulty: the farther the ball, the more the
goalkeeper must add a step and then a full spring to reach it. In Plan, both
"Diving" (U9-U15) and "Advanced Diving" (U16-U18) appear as explicit syllabus
items across the age bands — the same three techniques, trained at increasing
sophistication (see the [development framework](../framework/development-framework.md)).

## Robo's technique detail — near-body vs. well-away-from-body

Robo organizes the same material by proximity and gives the richest
mechanical breakdown. Its two near-body techniques correspond to the
Collapse Dive and Step Dive; its well-away technique corresponds to the
Extension/Power Dive.

### Near-body saves (Collapse Dive / one-step-and-dive)

Robo gives **two techniques for service near the body**:

1. **Collapse the dive.** Works best when the foot closest to the serve
   **slides away from the ball while the foot collapses**; simultaneously,
   **push the hands to the ball.** The timing of both movements together gets
   the goalkeeper to the ground quickly. (Robo's "Collapse dive" term:
   a diving technique for a ball served stiffly just to the side of the
   keeper — the closest foot slides away and collapses while the hands push
   to the ball.)

2. **Take one good step and dive** off the foot closest to the ball, and
   **push to the ball.**

In **both** near-body cases, the dive ends when **the ball is pulled into the
chest and the top leg is pulled into the body.** The end result of a good
dive: the goalkeeper lands **on his/her side** with the ball **tucked into
the chest.**

Two technical cues from Robo govern near-body diving by ball height:

- **Ball on the ground:** **bend or flex the knee closest to the ball when
  pushing off** — a bent knee helps get the body "down" to the ball. This is
  the "bend the knee closest to the ball" cue.
- **Ball rising up:** **push off the inside foot closest to the ball and push
  the hands across the body** to a good handling position behind the ball.
  This is the "push hands across" cue.

### Well-away-from-body saves (Extension / Power Dive)

For a ball played **well away from the body**, Robo's extended / "power"
technique:

- Take a **long step** with the foot closest to the ball, **followed by a
  quick short step with the same foot**; **on the second short step, take off
  to dive.**
- As the goalkeeper pushes off, **the top leg should also come across the
  body — this is the "power step"** (Robo's usage: the across-the-body motion
  of the top leg is what Robo calls the power step; compare Plan/ECNL's
  **Power Step**, the lateral push-off of the foot closest to the ball — both
  describe the same extension mechanic from slightly different angles).
- **At full stretch, the ball may need to be pinned into the ground on
  landing** — **top hand on top of the ball, bottom hand behind the ball.**
  When the body has completely finished landing, **pull the ball into the
  body.**

## Low vs. high saves

Hand shape and approach change with the height of the ball — a point Robo
makes in handling terms that applies directly to the diving save:

- **Low / on the ground:** get the hands near the ground; bend the knee
  closest to the ball to bring the body down (Robo). On the longest low
  saves, the **Three Hands** structure secures the ball — two hands plus the
  ground, one hand on top and one hand behind, with the ground as the third
  hand under the ball (Plan, ECNL; see [handling & catching](handling-catching.md)
  and the [glossary](../vocabulary.md)).
- **High / rising:** push off the inside foot closest to the ball and push
  the hands across the body to a good handling position behind the ball
  (Robo). On a high ball pushed over the bar, parry rather than catch (see
  parry section below and [aerial & crosses](aerial-crosses.md)).

For shots specifically threatening the area **over the wall** on free kicks,
Plan and ECNL both note: **people love to shoot over the wall**, so the
goalkeeper must **plan to dive that way to cover that area** — the wall's job
is only to make the shot difficult, not to stop it (see
[positioning & angles](positioning-angles.md)).

## Catch first — parry / deflection as a last resort

All three documents are explicit that the dive should finish in a **catch**
wherever possible, and that parrying or deflecting is a fallback, not a
preference:

- **Robo (principle):** "Catching with two hands is preferable to parrying or
  punching with one." Goalkeepers should be **trained to catch first and then
  parry or punch out of danger as a second and last resort.** A ball that is
  **held is the safest choice** a goalkeeper can make; a ball punched or
  parried can keep a team under continuous pressure. Good catching does not
  allow for second finishing opportunities.
- **Robo (when you must parry a high ball over the bar):** open the palm and
  **push underneath and through the back of the ball.** Often the safest play
  is to **parry with the hand on the opposite side of the body** (e.g., a ball
  on the left side of the body is parried with the right hand). This detail
  is covered in full under [aerial & crosses](aerial-crosses.md).

Where the ball cannot be held — driven, at full stretch, or struck too hard —
the goalkeeper deflects it to safety (over the bar, around the post, or wide
of the danger area) rather than spilling it into a dangerous central space.
The priority order is fixed: **catch the ball if you can; if you cannot,
deflect it out of danger.**

## Communicating the save

On every served/struck ball the goalkeeper claims or releases verbally —
**KEEPER** (two syllables; never allow simply yelling "Keep") when the
goalkeeper can make the save or handle the ball, **AWAY** when the goalkeeper
cannot make the save or wants a teammate to control it (Plan, ECNL). Full
communication detail lives in [psychology & mentality](psychology-mentality.md)
and the [communication syllabus](../framework/communication-syllabus.md).

## Coaching points — quick reference

- **Set first.** Stop moving and get into the Ready Position before the shot
  (Plan, ECNL). Stand up, hold, react — don't anticipate (Robo).
- **Push off the foot closest to the ball.** Universal across all three
  sources; the Power Step launches the dive (Plan, ECNL).
- **Collapse for near-body.** Closest foot slides away and collapses; hands
  push to the ball, timed together to get down quickly (Robo / Collapse Dive).
- **Or one step and dive** off the closest foot for a near-body ball (Robo /
  Step Dive).
- **Bend the knee closest to the ball** on a ball on the ground to get the
  body down (Robo).
- **Push the hands across the body** on a rising ball, off the inside foot
  closest to the ball (Robo).
- **Long-short-takeoff for the full-stretch dive.** Long step, quick short
  step (same foot), take off on the second; the top leg comes across — the
  power step (Robo / Extension-Power Dive).
- **Pin and pull.** At full stretch, pin the ball — top hand on top, bottom
  hand behind — then pull it into the body once landed (Robo).
- **Land on your side, ball tucked into the chest** (Robo).
- **Catch first; parry/deflect only as a last resort** (Robo).

## Drills

Diving drills — Collapse Dive, Step Dive, Extension/Power Dive, and the Power
Step that launches them — are collected in the [drill library](../drills/library.md),
which draws on the diving progressions in Plan and ECNL. Train these via the
two methods both Plan and Robo prescribe, Simple-to-Complex and
Whole-Part-Whole, ending in the most realistic match-condition replication
(see the [training methodology](../framework/training-methodology.md)).

  Goalkeeping*), Rob Walker, US National Goalkeeping Staff Coach. Primary
  source for technique detail: the collapse dive, one-step-and-dive,
  well-away "power"/extension technique (long step → short step → takeoff,
  top leg across the body), the bend-the-knee-closest-to-the-ball and
  push-hands-across cues, the pin-and-pull landing, land-on-your-side / ball-
  to-chest finish, the diving decision ("would staying upright leave the ball
  unabsorbed?"), and the catch-first / parry-as-last-resort principle.

  Birmingham United Soccer Association (BUSA). Source for the three-tier

  the Power Step, Set Yourself, and the KEEPER/AWAY claim-or-release rule.
- **Plan** — "Goalkeeper Plan 2015-2016." Source for the same three-tier
  diving ladder and Power Step definitions, the "Diving" / "Advanced Diving"
  age-band syllabus placement, the dive-over-the-wall note, and the
  Simple-to-Complex / Whole-Part-Whole training methods for diving.
