# Goalkeeping Glossary

The shared vocabulary of the `rasa.domain.soccer.goalkeeping` curriculum,
deduped and merged across all three source documents. A goalkeeper and
coach must share this language: a command is only useful if both ends of
it mean the same thing. Terms are grouped into three sections — **(1)
Technique**, **(2) Communication commands** (organized by the four-stage
communication syllabus), and **(3) Methodology**.

Where the three sources agree on a term, the definition is merged. Where
a term comes from only one source, or the sources differ, that is noted
inline. The technique vocabulary splits cleanly by lineage: Robo ("The
GK Trainer") supplies the prose-described technique cues (lead away,
power step, line of flight, concave hands, lock the ball, punt,
dropkick); ECNL and Plan supply the named, glossary-style technique
terms (Ready Position, Stalk, Steal Ground, the three named dives, the
four named catches) — and ECNL and Plan are near-identical on these, so
they are merged.

For the technique terms in context see the relevant
[syllabus](syllabus/) file; for the communication stages see the
[communication syllabus](framework/communication-syllabus.md); for the
methodology see the [training methodology](framework/training-methodology.md)
and the [development framework](framework/development-framework.md). Drills
that train these terms live in the [drill library](drills/library.md).

## 1. Technique terms

### Stance, set, and movement

**Goalie Stance / Ready Position** — Two hands down to the sides of the
body, knees bent, body in a squat position, feet shoulder-width apart, no
movement — just attention to the ball. *(ECNL + Plan, identical.)* See
[footwork & movement](syllabus/footwork-movement.md).

**Set / Set Yourself** — Stop moving and get into Ready Position; be
stationary, balanced, and ready for the shot. Robo describes "set" as
the state of being ready 4–6 yards off goal, achieved by stepping
sideways out of a sprint to slow momentum; ECNL/Plan name it "Set
Yourself" — stop moving and get into Ready Position before the shot. The
same concept under two labels.

**Stalk** — In a LOW Ready Position, the GK keeps their feet while
following the opponent with the ball; requires better-than-average
footwork to maintain a low center of gravity and steady balance during
movement. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**Steal Ground** — While an opponent is dribbling, the GK steps toward
them, taking ground while the ball is away from the feet; this lets the
GK cut angles and shorten the distance to the ball. On breakaways: take
steps forward ONLY once the opponent dribbles the ball away from their
foot. *(ECNL + Plan.)* See [1v1 & breakaway](syllabus/one-v-one-breakaway.md).

**Shuffling** — Moving across the goal while facing the field of play; a
footwork tool for establishing near-post/far-post angles. *(Robo.)*

**Sprinting (across the goal)** — Moving across the goal by sprinting
while keeping the shoulders turned so the GK always faces the field of
play; finished by stepping sideways out of the sprint to slow momentum
and get "set." *(Robo.)*

**Line of flight** — The path of the served or struck ball. Good
footwork keeps the body square to the line of flight. *(Robo.)*

### Footwork into the dive

**Lead away** — Pushing off and stepping toward a ball with the foot on
the side the ball is shot to (e.g., ball to the GK's right → "lead away"
with the right foot) when catching a ball played away from the body. The
first step is the most critical factor in the technique. *(Robo.)*

**Power step** — The across-the-body motion that powers an extended
dive. Robo: on a ball well away from the body, take a long step with the
foot closest to the ball, follow with a quick short step off the same
foot, then take off — as the GK pushes off, the top leg comes across the
body; that across-the-body motion is the "power step." ECNL/Plan name
the **Power Step** more narrowly as the launching action: when a shot is
taken, the foot closest to the ball steps laterally toward the ball; GKs
use this step in the process of diving. Both lineages treat it as the
spring source of the long dive. See [shot-stopping & diving](syllabus/shot-stopping-diving.md).

### Diving — the three named dives

ECNL and Plan grade the dive into three named techniques by save
distance; Robo describes the same techniques in prose (collapse vs.
power/extended) without the middle "Step Dive" label.

**Collapse Dive** — The short-diving technique: take the leg closest to
the ball out from under yourself to fall to the ground. Robo's prose
version: the foot closest to the serve slides away from the ball while
that foot collapses, and the GK simultaneously pushes the hands to the
ball; the timing of both movements gets the GK to the ground quickly.
*(All three — named in ECNL + Plan, described in Robo.)*

**Step Dive** — The medium-distance diving save: take an appropriate
step with the foot closest to the ball to fall to the ground. *(ECNL +
Plan; corresponds to Robo's "one good step and dive" off the foot
closest to the ball.)*

**Extension Dive / Power Dive** — The long diving save: using the power
step, the GK springs toward the ball. Robo's prose "power/extended"
technique: long step + quick short step off the same foot, take off, top
leg comes across the body; at full stretch the ball may be pinned into
the ground on landing (top hand on top of the ball, bottom hand behind
it) before being pulled into the body. *(All three.)*

### Handling — the four named catches

ECNL and Plan share an identical four-technique handling glossary. Robo
covers handling in prose ("concave" hands, "lock" the ball) rather than
with these names; both are listed below. See [handling & catching](syllabus/handling-catching.md).

**Basket Catch** — Create a basket with your hands and arms to securely
catch the ball, using your torso as a barrier. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**Scoop** — A Basket Catch performed by "scooping" the ball from a roll
on the ground, allowing the ball to roll up and into your arms. *(ECNL +
Plan.)*

**Contour Catch** — Two hands behind the ball when catching, forming a
diamond with the index fingers and thumbs. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**Three Hands** — Two hands plus the ground form a three-hand system when
handling the ball to the ground: one hand on top, one hand behind, while
the ground becomes the third hand under the ball. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**Concave hand position** — Robo's equivalent handling cue for catching
at the body: the fingers and thumb of each hand form a concave (cupped)
shape behind the ball; hands point downward on low balls and upward on
higher balls, with the body behind the ball, not to the side of it.
*(Robo.)*

**Lock the ball** — Securing the ball into the chest/body by finishing
the catch with footwork moving forward (or bending at the waist on high
balls), "killing" the momentum of the shot. *(Robo.)*

### Distribution

**Punt** — Kicking the ball out of the hand by dropping it in front of
the body and contacting behind and underneath it; the result is a high,
arching ball that "turns over" in mid-flight and extends its distance.
Build momentum with a long first step then several shorter steps to the
contact point. *(Robo.)* See [distribution](syllabus/distribution.md).

**Dropkick** — Kicking the ball out of the hand where the dropped ball is
contacted just off the ground, toes locked down and shoulders leaning
back, producing a ball driven on a low trajectory. *(Robo.)*

**Backpass** — A ball played back to the goalkeeper by a teammate,
requiring the GK to kick a moving ball off the ground (instep for smooth
passes driven away; side of the foot for balls coming in on the ground
and bouncing). Hold position as it arrives to assess pressure; meet it
early if contested. *(Robo.)* Distinct from the **(KEEPER'S) HOME**
*command* (see §2) that invites the back-pass.

**Back to Bar** — A syllabus item from U13 up: positioning/footwork
relative to the goal frame (recovering toward the crossbar). Used as a
training category in the age-band plans. *(Plan; named only here.)* See
[positioning & angles](syllabus/positioning-angles.md).

### Crosses, corners, and aerial situations

**Go Area / Green Area** — On corners and crosses, the area in front of
and around the keeper (marked green on the source diagrams): "your land
to step out and win balls in the air." *(ECNL + Plan.)* See
[aerial play & crosses](syllabus/aerial-crosses.md).

**Out-swinger** — A served ball (e.g., a corner or cross) more likely to
swing OUT, away from the keeper. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**In-swinger** — A served ball more likely to swing INTO the keeper.
*(ECNL + Plan.)*

### Failure terms

**Chip** — The ball goes over the GK's head and into the goal. On
breakaways, protecting against the chip is "the most important worry in
the beginning." *(ECNL + Plan.)*

**Meg (nutmeg)** — The ball goes in between the legs. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

### Decision concepts

**Can I win the ball?** — A consideration (not a commandment) the GK runs
through when a through ball or cross is delivered — weighing the total
distance to the ball, whether a teammate is closer, and obstacles in the
path — to decide whether to hold, come part-way and re-angle, or come all
the way and win it outright. *(Robo.)*

**Rule of Thumb (breakaways)** — The faster a player is dribbling, the
more likely they are to try to beat the GK 1v1; as a player slows down,
they prepare for the shot. *(ECNL + Plan.)*

## 2. Communication commands

The communication vocabulary is identical between ECNL and Plan and is
organized into a four-stage progression that scaffolds *what* a keeper
communicates as competence grows. Two cross-cutting rules govern all of
it: a **KEEPER** call is always two syllables (never allow the keeper to
simply yell "Keep"); and on every set piece the keeper claims or releases
the ball verbally — **KEEPER** or **AWAY** — no matter what, every single
time (including balls that fly well over the goal). A goalkeeper is never
a cheerleader. The full treatment is in the
[communication syllabus](framework/communication-syllabus.md).

### Stage One — claim the ball (self / the save)

**KEEPER** — A two-syllable call used every time the GK can make a save
or handle the ball; claims the ball for the keeper and signals control to
teammates and opponents. *(ECNL + Plan; Robo independently specifies the
same "KEEPER!" call when the GK commits to come win a ball — a command of
courtesy and tactics: teammates take cover, opponents know a challenge is
coming.)*

**AWAY** — A call used every time the GK cannot make a save or wants a
teammate to control the ball; releases the ball to a teammate. *(ECNL +
Plan.)*

### Stage Two — commands to an individual teammate

**(NAME) STEP / PRESSURE** — Tell an individual player to pressure the
ball and prevent the attacker from advancing or shooting; used when there
is space between the defender and the attacker.

**FORCE LEFT / FORCE RIGHT** — Tell a pressuring defender which way to
force the opponent. Left/Right is preferred to Inside/Outside unless the
ball is extremely wide; used when the defender is close to the attacker.

**(NAME) DROP** — Tell an individual player to run back to a more
defensive position. May also be used to address the entire team.

**TURN (LEFT/RIGHT)** — Let a teammate know they have time to turn with
the ball; the direction speeds up play and tells the player which side to
face.

**TIME** — Tell a teammate there is little pressure on the ball and they
have time to play it with their feet.

**MAN ON** — Tell a teammate they are under pressure or soon to be;
emphasize urgency.

**(KEEPER'S) HOME** — Tell a teammate to play the ball back to the
goalkeeper (invites a back-pass to start build-up).

### Stage Three — commands to the functional group (the back four)

**OPEN UP** — Tell a teammate to take an "open" body position to see as
much of the field as possible; used when the ball is played wide.

**DROP BACK** — Make the entire back line aware they need to run back
more defensively, quickly.

**PUSH UP** — Tell the entire defense to move up the field in unison; a
louder voice expresses more urgency.

**CONTAIN / DELAY** — Tell a teammate to stay in front of the ball and
slow the attacker so other players can recover; often paired with terms
addressing the recovering players.

**DOUBLE TEAM** — Tell a second defender to step to the ball to make
penetration harder; used when defenders outnumber attackers with proper
cover.

**(NAME) COVER** — Tell a teammate to drop behind another defender to
help cover; used with other commands to the first defender.

**KEEP OUTSIDE** — When the ball is wide, signal the defender to use body
shape to keep the ball wide or played negatively.

**WALL (#)** — Form a wall with the stated number of players, ten yards
from goal; the keeper then moves the wall into position.

**HOLD (LINE / SPOT / NAME)** — Defenders do not drop behind a certain
line/spot or the furthest-back defender, giving the keeper more control of
the space behind the defense; may also create an offside trap on free
kicks.

**(NAME) MARK (#)** — Tell a specific player to mark a specific opponent.

**GOAL SIDE** — Tell a player to defend tightly and stay on the goal-side
of their mark; used when defending free kicks alongside marking
instructions.

### Stage Four — advanced tactical instructions to the whole team

**(NAME) IS ON** — Tell an attacker that a certain player is able to
receive a pass.

**SWITCH** — Quickly send the ball to a player on the other side of the
field.

**SWING** — Drop the ball to a player so they can send it to the other
side; also tells the opposite-side player to get wide.

**(NAME) TUCK IN** — Tell a defender or group to fall in more centrally
and defend closer to goal.

**(NAME) RECOVER CENTRAL** — In a crossing situation, tell a defender to
run to the center of the field to defend a ball.

**(NAME) BACK POST** — Tell a specific player to recover to the back post
to mark a weak-side attacker running down the field.

**INTO TOUCH** — Used when the defense is not in position to build a
counter or play out of the back; express urgency and clear the ball out
of bounds, followed by instructions to the entire defense.

**SHIFT (RIGHT / LEFT)** — Get the entire team to move its defensive
shape toward the ball after the attacking team swings/switches the point
of attack.

## 3. Methodology terms

These name the *how* of training and assessing keepers. Robo and Plan
share the two coaching methods almost verbatim; "Testing" is named as a
discrete syllabus item only in Plan. See the
[training methodology](framework/training-methodology.md).

**Simple-to-Complex** — A coaching method that builds a skill/decision
from its constituent parts: the GK performs a series of parts comprising
the "whole," "grooves" it through repetition, and progresses through a
logical sequence of activities ending in the most realistic
match-condition replication of the skill/decision. *(Robo + Plan.)*

**Whole-Part-Whole** — A coaching method that begins with the full
("whole") skill under live conditions (live service, possibly teammates
and opponents), breaks it into sequenced parts for improvement, then
returns to the whole performance under the original conditions. Carries
an embedded assessment loop — observe the whole, break it down, re-observe
under the first conditions to gauge improvement. *(Robo + Plan.)*

**Testing** — An explicit syllabus item listed at the end of every
age-band objective list (U9–U12, U13–U15, U16–U18); keepers are formally
tested as part of the seasonal plan. The sources do not provide numeric
rubrics or pass/fail benchmarks — only that testing occurs. *(Plan;
named only here.)* See the [development framework](framework/development-framework.md).

## Sources

- **Robo** — "The GK Trainer" (*Developing a Training Program for
  Coaching Goalkeeping*), Rob Walker, US National Goalkeeping Staff
  Coach. Supplies the prose technique vocabulary: lead away, power
  step/extended-dive, line of flight, concave hands, lock the ball,
  shuffling/sprinting, set, punt, dropkick, backpass, "KEEPER!", "can I
  win the ball?", and both coaching methods (Simple-to-Complex,
  Whole-Part-Whole).
- **ECNL** — "ECNL Goalkeeping Curriculum & Handbook," First Edition,
  Birmingham United Soccer Association (BUSA). Supplies the named
  technique glossary (Ready Position, Set Yourself, Stalk, Steal Ground,
  Power Step, the three dives, the four catches, Chip/Meg, Go Area,
  in/out-swinger) and the full four-stage communication command set.
- **Plan** — "Goalkeeper Plan 2015–2016." Mirrors ECNL's technique and
  communication glossaries near-verbatim and uniquely names **Back to
  Bar** and **Testing** as age-band syllabus items, plus the two coaching
  methods shared with Robo.
