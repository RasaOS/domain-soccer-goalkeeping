# Training Methodology & Organization

This file defines HOW the goalkeeper is trained: the two delivery methods a
coach uses to build a skill or decision, the shape of a single session, the
organization of training across a week and a season, and how to chart a
keeper's progress over time. It is the operating manual that sits behind the
[development framework](development-framework.md) (which sets WHAT to train at
each age) and the [drill library](../drills/library.md) (which supplies the
activities). For the technique content the methods deliver, see the syllabus
files — e.g. [handling & catching](../syllabus/handling-catching.md),
[footwork & movement](../syllabus/footwork-movement.md),
[shot-stopping & diving](../syllabus/shot-stopping-diving.md). Term
definitions live in the [glossary](../vocabulary.md).

Robo and Plan agree almost word-for-word on the two methods and the
organization rules — both are reproduced faithfully below. ECNL is a
reference/vocabulary handbook: it does **not** prescribe session plans,
periodization, warm-ups, or load. Its only explicit developmental sequencing
is the four-stage communication progression (see
[communication syllabus](communication-syllabus.md)), so it informs this file
only on that point.

## The Two Delivery Methods

There are two coaching methods for training goalkeepers in an individual or
small-group setting. **Both methods END the same way — by returning the
keeper to the most realistic, match-condition replication of the skill or
decision.** That match-realistic finish is the non-negotiable point of either
method; a session that drills parts and never re-assembles them under live
conditions has not completed the method.

Choose Simple-to-Complex when the skill is new or being built from scratch
(you assemble parts UP into the whole). Choose Whole-Part-Whole when the keeper
already performs the skill but it needs diagnosis and repair (you start from
the whole, isolate the broken part, then re-assemble).

### Method 1 — Simple-to-Complex

Build the skill from its constituent parts and progress logically up to the
full match-realistic version.

1. **Decide the objective.** The coach and goalkeeper decide together what
   skill or decision to work on.
2. **Break it into parts.** The keeper performs a series of parts that, taken
   together, comprise the "whole" skill or decision.
3. **Groove it through repetition.** Repetitions are repeated to "groove" the
   keeper's ability to perform to the objective. Throughout, the coach must
   provide the opportunity for the keeper to **adapt kinesthetically** to the
   required movement, and must **challenge the keeper mentally and
   physically.**
4. **Finish match-realistic.** The method concludes when the keeper has
   completed a logical progression of activities **ending in the most
   realistic replication of the skill/decision under match conditions.**

### Method 2 — Whole-Part-Whole

Start from the full skill, diagnose it, repair the broken parts, then return
to the full skill under the original conditions.

1. **Define the skill/decision.** The coach and goalkeeper define what skill or
   decision needs to be made.
2. **Perform the WHOLE.** The keeper performs that skill in its most "whole"
   form — using **live service**, with the possible introduction of teammates
   and opponents.
3. **Observe and analyze.** Once the whole has been thoroughly performed, the
   coach observes and analyzes it. (This is the embedded assessment loop —
   the coach's read of the first whole is the baseline.)
4. **Break into PARTS.** The whole performance is broken down into little
   parts. Each part is **sequenced** so that the "whole" can be improved upon.
5. **Return to the WHOLE.** The final stage returns to the "whole" performance
   and **observes the skill/decision back under the conditions of the first
   whole performance** — measuring improvement against the original baseline.

Whole-Part-Whole carries a built-in evaluation cycle: observe the whole →
diagnose the parts → re-observe the whole under identical conditions. The
difference between the two whole observations is the keeper's improvement.

## Session Structure

Every training session follows the same three-part arc, which itself mirrors
Simple-to-Complex — basics first, the working block in the middle, a
match-realistic or load-bearing finish last.

1. **Warm-up — use the technique basics as a staple item.** Ready Position,
   footwork, and basic handling are the staple of the warm-up (Plan lists
   footwork first in every age band for exactly this reason — see
   [footwork & movement](../syllabus/footwork-movement.md)).
2. **Technique session.** The "meat" of the session — the working block where
   the chosen skill or decision is built or repaired via one of the two
   methods above.
3. **Finish: decision-making or fitness.** End the session with a little
   decision-making or fitness. This is where the technique block returns to a
   match-realistic replication.

**Emphasis by age** (per the [development framework](development-framework.md)):
younger goalkeepers are best served with an **emphasis on technique**; older
goalkeepers really need to focus on **technique, positioning AND fitness.**

The trainer must continually **balance emphasis between giving feedback and
keeping the goalkeeper(s) active** — too much standing and listening kills the
session; too little feedback wastes the reps.

## Organization

### Frequency & Duration by Age

Training load scales with age. (Plan runs all three age bands on a **Fall &
Spring (August–May)** calendar; the duration/frequency numbers below come from
Robo and are reproduced identically in Plan.)

| Age band | Frequency | Session length |
|---|---|---|
| U-12 through U-14 | Once or twice a week | ~45 minutes to an hour |
| U-15 through U-16 | Twice a week | A little over 60 minutes |
| U-17 through U-19 | 2–3 times per week | ~75–80 minutes |

For the full age-stage objectives behind these bands (Plan's U9–U12 / U13–U15 /
U16–U18 syllabus ladders, including which categories enter at each band), see
the [development framework](development-framework.md).

### Augmenting Team Training

**Goalkeeping training should always augment what is being done with the
team.** Pairing goalkeeping objectives with team training objectives is
**"always a good idea."** The keeper-specific session is not an island — it
serves the keeper's role in the team's game.

### Surveying the Field — Ground Safety

**Survey the working areas before training.** Ground that is **bumping, hard,
or uneven may not be safe or productive for good goalkeeping training** — a
keeper diving and landing repeatedly needs a surface that won't injure them or
distort the technique. Check the ground before you put a keeper on the floor.

### Equipment

Keep an **ample supply of cones, markers, flags, and a goal implement** in
daily training.

- **Cones / markers / flags** work best **at the beginning of a session** (to
  organize the warm-up and mark out areas) or for **"pull-out" purposes during
  a session** (isolating a part, marking a position, defining a zone).
- For the **"meat" of a session**, a **full-sized goal is generally preferable
  to any other equipment** — the keeper trains against the real frame they
  defend, not a substitute.

## Charting Progress

Charting a goalkeeper's progress over time is a good idea, by two
complementary means:

1. **Written journal.** Keep a written journal recording, **after each
   session:** the training completed, the coaching points stressed, and the
   performance of the goalkeeper. This is the running record of what was
   worked, what was emphasized, and how the keeper responded.
2. **Video.** Use a video recorder to help the goalkeeper understand their
   strengths and weaknesses in a particular area. Video also serves a
   motivational function: it can **encourage the "non-motivated" goalkeeper to
   train more regularly** — seeing the work on tape moves the keeper that words
   alone don't reach.

Plan additionally makes **"Testing"** an explicit syllabus item at the end of
every age band's objective list (U9–U12, U13–U15, U16–U18) — keepers are tested
as part of the seasonal plan. Note: Plan does **not** supply numeric rubrics,
pass/fail benchmarks, or selection criteria for that testing — only that
testing occurs. Where formal evaluation detail is needed, it has to be built
locally per deployment; the sources stop at "test, journal, and video."
