# Age / Level Development Framework

This is the spine that tells a coach **what to emphasize, how often to
train, for how long, and how to test it** at each stage of a keeper's
development. It is the *when* and *to whom* of the domain; the
[syllabus](../syllabus/README.md) is the *what* and the
[training methodology](training-methodology.md) is the *how*.

Two of the three sources carry an explicit age model, and they agree on
the through-line: **younger keepers are best served by an emphasis on
technique; older keepers focus on technique, positioning, and fitness.**

- **Robo** ("The GK Trainer," Rob Walker) frames the model around
  **training load** — three bands (U-12 through U-14, U-15 through U-16,
  U-17 through U-19) with rising frequency and duration, and the
  technique → technique+positioning+fitness shift as the keeper ages.
- **Plan** (Goalkeeper Plan 2015–2016) frames the model around
  **syllabus content** — three bands (U9–U12, U13–U15, U16–U18) run on a
  **Fall & Spring (August–May)** calendar, each with an explicit list of
  what is coached, and each ending in **"Testing."**
- **ECNL** (BUSA) carries no age/load model. Its only developmental
  sequencing is the four-stage communication ladder, which is covered in
  the [communication syllabus](communication-syllabus.md), not here.

Robo's bands and Plan's bands do not line up edge-to-edge — Robo's
youngest band runs U-12→U-14 while Plan's runs U9→U12. The unified model
below resolves them into three stages keyed off Plan's content bands,
and carries Robo's load figures verbatim, flagging where the two
disagree on a boundary.

## The Unified Stage Model at a Glance

| Stage | Plan band | Robo load band | Emphasis | Frequency & duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | U9–U12 | U-12 → U-14 | Technique-first | Once or twice a week, ~45 min to an hour |
| **Development** | U13–U15 | U-15 → U-16 | Technique + early positioning/tactics | Twice a week, a little over 60 min |
| **Advanced** | U16–U18+ | U-17 → U-19 | Technique + positioning + fitness | 2–3 times per week, ~75–80 min |

> **Boundary caveat.** Robo's load figures are keyed to his own bands
> (U-12→14, U-15→16, U-17→19); Plan's content lists are keyed to U9–12,
> U13–15, U16–18. They overlap but are not identical — e.g. a U13/U14
> keeper sits in Plan's *Development* content band but still inside
> Robo's *youngest* load band (~45–60 min, once or twice weekly). Read
> the duration as the load *at the older end of each stage*, and scale
> down for the younger end. This is a faithful merge of two sources, not
> a single authored standard.

Both sources agree on a fixed rule that cuts across all stages: **goalkeeping
training should always augment what the team is doing — pairing
goalkeeping objectives with team training objectives is "always a good
idea."** Calendar (Plan): **Fall & Spring, August–May**, for all three
bands.

## Stage 1 — Foundation (U9–U12)

**Calendar:** Fall & Spring (August–May).
**Frequency & duration (Robo, U-12→U-14):** train **once or twice a week
for about 45 minutes to an hour**.
**Emphasis:** **technique-first.** Robo is explicit that "younger
goalkeepers best served with an emphasis on technique"; positioning and
fitness are *not* the priority here.

This is where the goalkeeping essentials get grooved before any tactical
or fitness load is layered on. Plan's U9–U12 syllabus:

- **Footwork** — listed first, the staple of every warm-up. See
  [footwork & movement](../syllabus/footwork-movement.md).
- **Catching** (basic technique) — Basket Catch, Scoop, Contour Catch,
  Three Hands. See [handling & catching](../syllabus/handling-catching.md).
- **Footskills** — basic play with the feet. See
  [playing with feet](../syllabus/playing-with-feet.md).
- **Judgment of balls** — Air, Ground, and **50/50** (the first
  introduction of breakaway decision-making). See
  [aerial & crosses](../syllabus/aerial-crosses.md) and
  [1v1 & breakaway](../syllabus/one-v-one-breakaway.md).
- **Diving** — Collapse Dive / Step Dive / Extension Dive ladder. See
  [shot-stopping & diving](../syllabus/shot-stopping-diving.md).
- **Positioning / Angles** — introduced, but secondary to technique. See
  [positioning & angles](../syllabus/positioning-angles.md).
- **Testing** — the stage checkpoint (see *Testing* below).

Note what is **absent** at this stage in Plan's list: no Communication,
no Distribution, no Set Plays, no Fitness as standalone items. Distribution
in particular is **not listed for U9–U12** — it enters at U13. This is
deliberate: the foundation is hands, feet, and reading the ball, not
tactics or conditioning.

Robo's session shape supports this — sessions run warm-up (technique
basics as the staple) → technique session → a little decision-making or
fitness to close, with the technique block dominant at this age.

## Stage 2 — Development (U13–U15)

**Calendar:** Fall & Spring (August–May).
**Frequency & duration (Robo, U-15→U-16):** **twice a week, a little over
60 minutes.**
**Emphasis:** technique remains central, but the syllabus **expands into
tactics, communication, distribution, and the first standalone fitness
work.**

Plan's U13–U15 syllabus adds five new categories on top of the foundation:

- **Footwork** — still first, still a warm-up staple.
- **Catching** — continued technical development.
- **Diving** — continued.
- **Judging Balls** — continued (Air / Ground / 50/50).
- **Positioning / Angles** — now a fuller emphasis than at foundation.
- **Communication** *(new)* — the four-stage ladder begins in earnest.
  See [communication syllabus](communication-syllabus.md).
- **Back to Bar** *(new)* — recovery footwork/positioning to defend the
  crossbar. See [positioning & angles](../syllabus/positioning-angles.md).
- **Distribution** *(new)* — punting, dropkicking, throwing, backpass
  handling. See [distribution](../syllabus/distribution.md). (Robo notes
  these "take several years and many matches to get a good feel for" —
  consistent with introducing them mid-development, not at foundation.)
- **Fitness** *(new)* — standalone conditioning enters here.
- **Set Plays** *(new)* — corners and free-kick work. See
  [aerial & crosses](../syllabus/aerial-crosses.md).
- **Testing** — the stage checkpoint.

This is the band where the position broadens from pure shot-stopping into
organizing and distributing. Robo's load (twice weekly, 60+ min) gives the
extra session time the wider syllabus needs.

---

## Stage 3 — Advanced (U16–U18+)

**Calendar:** Fall & Spring (August–May).
**Frequency & duration (Robo, U-17→U-19):** **2–3 times per week for about
75–80 minutes.**
**Emphasis:** the full triad. Both sources state it plainly — **older
goalkeepers really need to focus on technique, positioning, AND fitness.**

Plan's U16–U18 syllabus is the U13–U15 list with most items promoted to
**Advanced**:

- **Footwork**
- **Advanced Handling / Catching**
- **Advanced Diving**
- **Judging Balls**
- **Advanced Positioning / Angles**
- **Advanced Communication** — toward Stage Four of the
  [communication ladder](communication-syllabus.md) (whole-team tactical
  organization).
- **Back to Bar**
- **Advanced Distribution** — playing out of the back, switching the
  point of attack (SWITCH / SWING / INTO TOUCH). See
  [distribution](../syllabus/distribution.md) and
  [playing with feet](../syllabus/playing-with-feet.md).
- **Advanced Fitness**
- **Advanced Set Plays**
- **Testing** — the stage checkpoint.

The highest training load lives here (up to 3 sessions/week, ~75–80 min)
because the keeper is now carrying technique, positioning, AND fitness
simultaneously. Robo's long-horizon view applies most directly at this
stage: "competent goalkeeping can be attained in the short term" but
"high-level (world-class) goalkeeping" is achieved "over a longer period"
— the holding-and-reacting and distribution skills that mature here "take
years of practice."

## Testing — The Per-Stage Checkpoint

Plan closes **every** age band's syllabus with the same item: **Testing.**
It is the developmental checkpoint that confirms a keeper is ready to carry
the next stage's syllabus and load.

Plan lists "Testing" as a category but does not spell out its battery in
the extracted material — so the *content* of a test is drawn from the
syllabus the keeper has just covered (footwork, catching, diving, judging
balls, positioning at foundation; plus communication, distribution, set
plays, fitness as the bands progress). Test what was coached.

For the **mechanism** of testing and progress-tracking, both Robo and Plan
prescribe the same tools, covered in the
[training methodology](training-methodology.md):

- **Charting progress** — a written journal kept after each session,
  recording the training completed, the coaching points stressed, and the
  keeper's performance over time.
- **Video** — recording to help the keeper see strengths and weaknesses in
  a specific area (and to motivate the "non-motivated" keeper to train
  more regularly).
- **Whole-Part-Whole's embedded loop** — the method itself is an
  assessment cycle: observe the whole skill under match-realistic
  conditions, break it into parts to improve, then re-observe under the
  original whole conditions to gauge improvement.

Treat "Testing" as the gate at each stage transition: a Foundation keeper
who tests clean on footwork/catching/diving/judgment/positioning is ready
to absorb Development's communication, distribution, and set-play content;
a Development keeper who tests clean is ready for the Advanced load and the
"Advanced" promotions of each category.

## The Technique → Positioning → Fitness Shift

The single most load-bearing principle in both age models is the emphasis
shift across the three stages. Stated by Robo and echoed by Plan:

> **Younger goalkeepers are best served with an emphasis on technique.
> Older goalkeepers really need to focus on technique, positioning, and
> fitness.**

Read as a progression:

1. **Foundation (U9–U12):** technique is the near-exclusive focus.
   Positioning is introduced but secondary; fitness is not a standalone
   item; distribution is absent.
2. **Development (U13–U15):** technique stays central; positioning,
   communication, distribution, set plays, and the first standalone
   fitness work layer on.
3. **Advanced (U16–U18+):** technique + positioning + fitness carried
   together, with most syllabus items promoted to "Advanced," at the
   highest training load.

The corresponding load curve (Robo): once–twice/week × ~45–60 min →
twice/week × 60+ min → 2–3×/week × ~75–80 min. Frequency, duration, and
breadth of syllabus all rise together as the keeper matures.
